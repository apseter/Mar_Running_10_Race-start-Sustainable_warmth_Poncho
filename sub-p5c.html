Executive Summary

Race series are the B2B growth accelerator. A single race director controls one race per year. A single series operator controls 8–35+ races per year. Converting one series decision-maker is equivalent to converting 8–35 individual race directors. The volume potential is transformative: Rock ’n’ Roll alone could consume 100,000–200,000 ponchos annually, representing $250K–$700K in revenue from ONE account.

This document builds the enterprise sales strategy for 5 priority race series, a sustainability intelligence framework for researching each series’ environmental commitments and sponsor landscape, an 8-section partnership proposal template for series-level deals, a plan for 5 industry events where series decision-makers can be reached in person, partnerships with 5 environmental organizations that amplify the brand’s credibility, and a Race Sustainability Calculator that turns race directors into self-qualifying leads.

2. Sustainability Intelligence: 5-Source Research Framework

Before approaching any series, complete a sustainability dossier. This research takes 2–3 hours per series and provides: the RIGHT messaging for the first outreach, specific sustainability claims to reference in the proposal, sponsor intelligence for the Sponsor-Funded pitch, and competitive intelligence on what they currently use at the start line.

Source What to Research How This Informs the Pitch
Series website Check for: dedicated sustainability page, environmental commitments (zero waste, carbon neutral, plastic-free), partnerships with environmental organizations, specific waste reduction metrics or goals Sustainability page exists = CHAMPION. General eco-mention = PRAGMATIC. No mention = SKEPTIC. Same segmentation framework as P5A Section 2 but applied at the series level.
Annual/ESG reports For corporate-owned series (Rock ’n’ Roll/Ironman, Spartan): check the parent company’s ESG or sustainability report. Often available at the corporate website under Investors or About sections. ESG reports reveal: specific environmental KPIs (waste diversion targets, plastic reduction goals), budget allocated to sustainability, and the decision-maker responsible for sustainability initiatives (often a VP or Director of Sustainability).
Sponsor analysis Identify the series’ title and presenting sponsors. For each: check if the sponsor has their own sustainability commitments (many corporations now require their sponsored events to align with ESG goals). Sponsor sustainability alignment creates a three-way opportunity: the sponsor funds the compostable poncho (per P3C Section 4 Sponsor-Funded tier), the series gets free ponchos, and the brand gets volume. Research sponsor ESG pages: search “[Sponsor Name] sustainability report” or “[Sponsor Name] ESG.”
Social media + press Search: “[Series Name] sustainability” or “[Series Name] environment” or “[Series Name] waste.” Check: Twitter/X, Instagram, race-specific Facebook groups, Runner’s World coverage, local press. Look for: race participants complaining about waste (“Saw so many garbage bags littering the course”), race promoting sustainability efforts (“We diverted 80% of race waste this year”), or media coverage of environmental issues at the event.
Competitor product usage Determine: does the series currently use Heatsheets, generic garbage bags, or nothing at the start line? Has the series tried compostable alternatives before? If currently using Heatsheets: pitch as a SWITCH (compostable poncho replaces non-compostable blankets at competitive pricing). If garbage bags: pitch as an UPGRADE (from trash to branded sustainability). If nothing: pitch as a NEW SERVICE (enhance runner experience + create sustainability story).

2.1 Sustainability Dossier Template

For each target series, compile a 1-page dossier: SERIES: [name]. PARENT COMPANY: [name]. SUSTAINABILITY TIER: Champion / Pragmatic / Skeptic. KEY SUSTAINABILITY INITIATIVES: [list]. CURRENT START-LINE SOLUTION: garbage bags / Heatsheets / other / unknown. TITLE SPONSORS: [list with sustainability commitment Y/N]. KEY DECISION-MAKER: [name, title, LinkedIn URL, email]. RECOMMENDED APPROACH: lead with sustainability / lead with value / lead with proof. NOTES: [any relevant press coverage, social media mentions, or insider intelligence]. Store dossiers in CRM as notes attached to the series contact record.

3. Series Partnership Proposal: 8-Section Template

The series partnership proposal is a professional 8–12 page document that positions the brand as a strategic sustainability partner, not just a product vendor. Race series don’t buy ponchos — they buy sustainability solutions that improve runner experience, generate sponsor value, and create positive media coverage. The proposal must speak to ALL THREE value dimensions.

Section Content
Cover page Series logo + brand logo. Title: “Sustainability Partnership Proposal: Compostable Warmth Ponchos for [Series Name].” Subtitle: “Eliminating start-line waste across [X] races, [Y] runners.” Date and confidentiality notice.
Executive summary (1 page) 3–4 paragraphs: The problem (start-line waste across the series), the solution (BPI-certified compostable poncho with co-branding and composting logistics), the opportunity (first major race series to eliminate start-line waste = PR + ESG impact), and the ask (series-wide partnership for [Year]).
The waste problem (1 page) Quantify start-line waste for the series: [X] races × [Y] runners per race × estimated [Z] garbage bags/Heatsheets used = [W] lbs of LDPE waste to landfill per year. Include: race-specific waste data from P0A if available, industry-wide data from P0A Section 2 (35K–45K garbage bags at a major marathon, 700–1,200 lbs LDPE waste per race). Visualization: before/after photo (garbage bags littering course vs. compostable ponchos in compost bins).
Product overview (1 page) Product specs, BPI certification, thermal performance (FLIR images), tear-away mechanism, sizing/fit. Include: 1–2 pilot case study summaries (“At [Race Name], 75% of ponchos were collected and composted, diverting [X] lbs from landfill”). Photo of the co-branded poncho with a placeholder series logo.
Partnership structure (1–2 pages) Volume pricing: series-wide pricing tier based on total annual volume (per P3E Section 6 volume targets). Co-branding: series logo + individual race logo + optional sponsor logo per event. Composting logistics: brand coordinates composting partners in each race city (per P4D network). Data + reporting: post-race composting reports for every event, annual sustainability impact roll-up. Exclusivity option: brand is the exclusive compostable poncho supplier for the series (in exchange for volume commitment).
Financial model (1 page) Scenario table: Unit price at different volume tiers (25K, 50K, 100K units/year). Total annual cost to the series. Compare to: current spend on Heatsheets or garbage bags (if known). Sponsor-funded option: “If [Sponsor Name] funds the ponchos at $[X]/unit, the series cost is $0.” ROI: sustainability PR value, runner satisfaction improvement, sponsor activation opportunity.
Implementation timeline (1 page) Month 1–2: Agreement signed, co-brand templates created for all race logos. Month 3–4: Production of first-season ponchos. Month 5: Delivery begins for first races of the season. Month 6–12: Execute across all races, collect data, produce reports. Month 12: Annual review + Year 2 planning.
Appendices Appendix A: Pilot case study (full version per P5B Section 6). Appendix B: BPI certification documentation. Appendix C: Material technical specifications. Appendix D: Composting partner network coverage map (P4D Section 5). Appendix E: Runner satisfaction survey data from pilots.

3.1 Proposal Customization

EVERY proposal must be customized for the specific series. Generic proposals are ignored by enterprise buyers. Customize: (1) Cover page with the series’ logo (request permission or use from their public media kit). (2) Waste problem section with series-specific data (total races × total runners × waste estimate). (3) Co-brand mockup with the series logo on a poncho rendering. (4) Financial model with the series’ estimated volume and pricing tier. (5) Composting logistics section referencing specific cities where the series hosts events. (6) Reference any series-specific sustainability goals from the dossier. TIME: 4–8 hours per customized proposal. COST: $0 (founder’s time + Canva design). VALUE: a custom proposal that shows “I did my homework on your series” opens doors that generic emails cannot.

4. Industry Events: Decision-Maker Access Points

Race series decision-makers attend a small number of industry events each year. These events are the highest-probability environments for initiating enterprise relationships — a face-to-face conversation at a conference is 10× more effective than a cold email for reaching a VP at Ironman Group or a director at Spartan.

Event Timing Location Strategy
The Running Event (TRE) Annual (typically December) Austin, TX THE premier running industry trade show. Attended by: race directors, running retail, running brands, media. 2,000+ attendees. Cost: $200–$400 registration. PRIORITY 1 event. Actions: attend, walk the expo floor, network, hand out sample kits + business cards. If budget allows: book a booth ($1,500–$3,000) for maximum visibility.
Running USA Conference Annual (typically February–March) Various US cities Running USA is the industry association for the road running industry. Conference attendees: race directors, series operators, running industry executives. 500+ attendees. Cost: $300–$500 (member) or $500–$700 (non-member). Actions: attend seminars, participate in networking events, present at the sustainability track (submit a speaker proposal: “How Compostable Ponchos Are Changing Race-Day Waste”).
RRCA National Convention Annual (typically April) Various US cities Road Runners Club of America annual gathering. Attendees: local running club leaders, race directors, community race organizers. More grassroots than TRE/Running USA. 300–500 attendees. Cost: $200–$350. Actions: network with small/community race directors (Tier 3 per P0C), distribute sample kits, speak on sustainability panel.
Green Sports Alliance Summit Annual (typically varies) Various US cities Focused on sustainability in sports. Attendees: sustainability directors from sports organizations, facilities, and brands. 300+ attendees. Cost: $400–$700. Actions: position the brand as a sustainability innovation leader, connect with sustainability directors at race series and large event organizations, explore partnership with Green Sports Alliance (Section 6).
Pack Expo Annual (October/November) Chicago or Las Vegas Packaging industry trade show. Relevant for: meeting compostable packaging suppliers (already covered in P3A), but ALSO for meeting sustainability-focused brands and retailers who might carry or co-promote the poncho. Actions: walk the compostable packaging section, identify new material suppliers, network with eco-brand founders.

4.1 Event Preparation Checklist

Before EVERY industry event: (1) Research attendee list (if published) and identify target contacts. Pre-schedule meetings via email: “I’ll be at [Event] next week — would love to grab 15 minutes to show you what we’re building.” (2) Prepare: 50 business cards, 10–20 B2B sample kits, 5 customized co-brand mockups for target series (printed on card stock), 1 poncho to wear as a conversation starter. (3) Prepare: 30-second elevator pitch (“We make a BPI-certified compostable warmth poncho for race start lines. It replaces garbage bags, composts after the race, and we handle all the logistics. [Race Name] just piloted it and composted 450 lbs of waste.”). (4) Set goals: X meetings booked, Y sample kits distributed, Z email sign-ups. (5) Follow up within 48 hours of the event with everyone met: personalized email referencing the conversation + attached sample kit offer.

A Green Sports Alliance endorsement proves the brand is a legitimate sustainability player. A 1% for the Planet membership proves the founder’s commitment is genuine, not performative. These partnerships address the #1 B2B objection for sustainability products: “Is this real or just greenwashing?”

6. Race Sustainability Calculator

The calculator is a digital B2B lead generation tool hosted at brand.com/calculator. A race director enters their runner count and current start-line solution, and the tool instantly shows: waste generated today, waste diverted with compostable ponchos, CO₂ avoided, and estimated cost with comparison to Heatsheets. The output is a shareable infographic card the director can show their board or sponsors. Behind the scenes: every calculator use captures an email for the CRM pipeline.

Element Description Notes
Input: Runner count Race director enters the total number of registered runners (or expected finishers) Numeric input field. Default: 5,000. Range: 100–100,000.
Input: Current start-line solution Dropdown: “Garbage bags,” “Heatsheets/Mylar blankets,” “Old clothing donations,” “Nothing,” “Other” Determines the comparison baseline for waste calculations.
Output: Waste generated (current) Runner count × waste-per-unit factor. Garbage bags: 0.04 lbs/bag (LDPE). Heatsheets: 0.02 lbs/blanket (LDPE/PET). Old clothing: 0.5 lbs/item (textile). Nothing: 0 lbs. Displays: “Your race currently generates approximately [X] lbs of start-line waste.”
Output: Waste diverted (with poncho) Runner count × 0.06 lbs/poncho (poncho weight) × collection rate (70% default) = lbs diverted from landfill Displays: “With compostable ponchos, you could divert [X] lbs from landfill to composting.”
Output: CO₂e avoided Waste diverted (tons) × 0.5 metric tons CO₂e/ton (conservative estimate per P4D Section 4) Displays: “Equivalent to [X] metric tons of CO₂ emissions avoided.”
Output: Cost estimate Runner count × unit price ($3.50–$5.00 based on volume tier from P3C Section 4). Show: total cost, cost per runner, comparison to Heatsheets cost ($4.55/blanket × runners) Displays: “Estimated cost: $[X] total ($[Y] per runner). Compared to Heatsheets: $[Z] total ($4.55/runner). You save $[W].”
Output: Sustainability impact summary Branded infographic card: “If [Race Name] switched to compostable ponchos: [X] lbs diverted, [Y] CO₂e avoided, [Z] runners kept warm sustainably.” Downloadable as PDF. Shareable on social media. Include: “Ready to make it happen? Request a sample kit and co-brand mockup: brand.com/b2b” with CTA button.
Technical build React component (P5C artifact) or simple HTML/JS page hosted at brand.com/calculator. Mobile-responsive. No login required. Email capture optional (“Enter your email to save your results and receive a custom proposal”). Build as a lightweight web tool. The calculator itself is a B2B lead generation tool: race directors who use it are self-qualifying. Capture: email + race name + runner count for CRM pipeline (with consent per GDPR/CAN-SPAM).

6.1 Calculator as Sales Tool

USE IN MEETINGS: during a B2B sales call (P5A Section 6), share the calculator screen and enter the race’s actual runner count live: “Let me show you what this looks like for [Race Name] with [X] runners.” The live calculation creates an “aha moment” as the race director sees their specific waste impact quantified. USE IN OUTREACH: include a link in Email 1 (P5A Section 3): “Curious how much start-line waste your race generates? Try our calculator: brand.com/calculator.” Self-service engagement — the race director qualifies themselves by using the tool. USE AT EXPOS: display the calculator on a tablet at the booth. Let expo attendees enter their race’s data and see the results. Print the infographic card for them to take home.

8. Conclusion: Series Deals Are the Path to Scale

Individual race directors build the foundation. Series deals build the business. One REVEL partnership covers 8–10 races and 30,000–50,000 units per year. One Rock ’n’ Roll deal covers 15+ US races and 100,000+ units. These are the deals that transform the brand from a promising startup into a viable business with predictable, recurring revenue.

The approach is methodical: research before contact, customize before sending, prove before proposing. No series operator will sign a 100,000-unit deal based on an email. They will sign it based on: a custom proposal that quantifies THEIR waste problem, a pilot case study that proves the product works at a comparable race, an in-person relationship built at The Running Event or Running USA Conference, and a sustainability calculator that shows THEIR board the ROI. The first 2–3 pilot case studies (P5B) are the foundation. Everything in this section builds on that foundation to reach enterprise scale.
