Sub-Project P0C | Market Research & Concept Validation | Weeks 3–6

The Complete B2B Intelligence Playbook for Compostable Marathon Start-Line Products

March 2026 | Confidential

Executive Summary

A compostable warmth poncho enters a market with zero branded competition at the start line, 230,000+ lbs of annual clothing waste at the top three US marathons alone, and a race industry increasingly pressured by sponsors and certifiers to go green. This report covers database building across seven platforms, 53 prioritized cold-weather races, procurement timelines and budgets, Mom Test interview frameworks, sustainability pain points, sponsor dynamics, registration add-on mechanics, B2B sales cycle benchmarks, pilot program design, and a trade show calendar.

1.1 Race Organizational Structure

Sustainability coordinator is NOT a standard role at most race organizations. Sustainability decisions go to the race director or operations lead directly — strategically advantageous for sales since there is no complex org chart to navigate.

1.2 LinkedIn Search Strategy

Search for people with titles “Race Director,” “Executive Race Director,” “Director of Race Operations” filtered to the US with keywords “running,” “marathon,” or “endurance.” Follow Running USA, RunSignUp, and Race Directors HQ pages to surface ecosystem connections.

3. How Race Directors Buy: Timelines, Budgets & Decision Authority

3.1 Procurement Timeline

For a fall race in October, sales conversations should begin January–March. RRCA guidelines specify 6–12 months advance planning. Consumable start-line supplies ordered 2–4 months before race day. Large events finalize procurement 6+ months out.

3.2 Decision Authority & Budget by Race Size

Race Size Typical Budget Decision Maker Contact Method
Small race (<1,000) $5K–$10K Race Director (sole decider) Generic info@ email on website
Mid-size (1,000–5,000) $30K–$50K RD + small committee; board for nonprofits Staff page with individual emails
Large (10,000+) $500K–$5M+ Operations/procurement lead + RD + sponsors Department emails; LinkedIn for individuals

3.3 Start-Line Product Cost Benchmarks

Generic mylar: $0.50–$0.75/unit. Heatsheets: ~$1.43 retail / $0.80–1.20 at scale. Cheap PE ponchos: $0.40–1.00/unit. Throwaway clothing: free to race but creates massive logistics burden. Garbage bags: free; universal default.

4. Customer Discovery Interviews: Mom Test for Race Directors

4.1 Questions That Pass the Mom Test

Question Why It Works
Walk me through how you manage the start line on a cold-weather race morning. Opens with their reality, not your product
What happened with waste collection at your last event? Specific past behavior, not hypothetical future
How are you currently dealing with discarded clothing at the start line? Maps existing workflow and pain points
What’s the most expensive or frustrating part of your post-race cleanup? Identifies budget/operations pain
Tell me about the last time a sponsor asked about your sustainability practices. Tests whether sponsor pressure is real or imagined
What’s the last new vendor product you adopted? How did you evaluate it? Reveals actual purchasing behavior
Who besides you is involved in purchasing decisions for event supplies? Maps the decision-making unit

4.2 Format, Length & Incentives

In-person at events is the gold standard. Target 25–30 minutes. Best incentive: value exchange (share aggregated sustainability findings). Commitment currencies that validate real interest: time (schedule follow-up), money (LOI), reputation (introduce other RDs), risk (willing to pilot).

5. Sustainability Pain Points: 230,000+ lbs of Start-Line Waste

Jackets, shirts, hats, gloves — literally thousands of pieces of clothing littering the ground for blocks. — Observer, post-start Chicago Marathon

There was a lot of trash this year because of the ponchos and those one-piece suits they buy at Home Depot. — Judy Pitasi, Boston Marathon clothing collection captain

The strongest pitch isn’t environmental — it’s operational. A poncho that eliminates 200+ clothing-sorting volunteers, 320 Goodwill bins, and charity coordination partnerships is a compelling operations upgrade that happens to also be green.

London Marathon’s 2019 trial of 500 reusable capes was discontinued because the environmental impact of production/washing outweighed waste reduction — creating an opening specifically for compostable single-use products.

The start line is the last unbranded moment in the marathon experience. Heatsheets owns the finish line at ~95% penetration. No equivalent branded product exists at the start. A compostable poncho with sponsor co-branding at $2–5/unit for 30K–50K runners = $75K–$250K sponsorship package.

7. Registration Add-On Model: RunSignUp & ACTIVE.com

RunSignUp: Add-Ons are race-director-controlled, purchasable during registration. Standard 6% + $1 fee. No third-party auto-listing mechanism — you must sell to race directors who configure the add-on. ACTIVE.com offers superior third-party vendor portal with automatic revenue share. Race Roster (4,000+ organizers) offers similar features.

Estimated conversion rate for registration add-ons: 15–30%. At $5–7 poncho add-on for a 10,000-runner race = 1,500–3,000 units per race through registration alone.

8. B2B Sales Cycle: Industry Benchmarks

New vendor relationships: 2–6 months from first contact to PO. Existing relationships reorder 3–6 months before event. Per-runner pricing model dominates. Free sample kits are standard practice. Industry events (TRE, Running USA) are essential for lead generation.

Heatsheets’ model is the template: branded warming blankets as advertising space. Their recycling program started as a pilot with 6 events in 2008, validated, then formalized the Trex partnership in 2011. Two-phase approach: prove at small scale, then validate at marquee event.

9. Pilot Program Design

9.1 Three-Tier Structure

Tier 1 (Free Sample Kit): 50–100 ponchos for evaluation. Tier 2 (Subsidized Pilot): 50% discount for 2–3 races with data collection commitment. Tier 3 (Full Partnership): At cost or free for marquee races in exchange for co-marketing + case study. Target: 3,000–10,000 total pilot ponchos.

9.2 Data Collection Framework

Category Metrics to Collect
Waste / Environmental Total poncho waste weight, % diverted, composting timeline, lbs per runner vs mylar baseline
Runner Feedback Warmth rating 1–10, comfort, ease of use, NPS, preference vs garbage bag/Goodwill
Operational Distribution time/runner, volunteer training, storage, tear/failure rate, collection efficiency
Financial Cost/poncho vs mylar, sponsor revenue opportunity, total cost incl disposal logistics
Visual / Marketing Photos in corral, social mentions, brand visibility in race photography, UGC content

Success criteria: runner satisfaction comparable to or better than mylar, measurable waste diversion improvement, race director willing to provide testimonial, total cost competitive when factoring sustainability marketing value.

11. Conclusion: Five Strategic Insights

1. The start line is the last unbranded moment in the marathon experience — a category-defining opportunity.

2. Sustainability is sponsor-mandated, not optional — Bank of America requires compostable at Chicago; Schneider Electric is Boston’s sustainability partner.

3. The strongest pitch is operational simplification — eliminating 200+ volunteers and 320 bins is more compelling than environmental messaging alone.

4. REVEL series is the ideal beachhead — cold mountain starts, existing warmth products, one partnership = multiple events.
