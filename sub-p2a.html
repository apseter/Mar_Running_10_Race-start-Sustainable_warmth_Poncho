5. Field Trial Protocol: 8+ Tester Corral Simulation

This is the most critical P2A activity. Lab-measured CLO values confirm thermal physics, but only real-world field testing confirms the RUNNER EXPERIENCE: does the poncho feel warm, comfortable, and easy to use at 6 AM in a cold corral? The following protocol generates the data needed for both the final material decision (task 2.07) and the B2B pitch deck (P1D Slide 8).

Element Specification
Testers Recruit 8–12 runners: mix of body sizes (S–XXL), genders, experience levels (first-timer to ultra). All must have run at least 1 race. Pay: free poncho + $25 gift card per test session.
Conditions Target: 35–45°F (2–7°C), wind 5–15 mph, overcast or light rain. If natural conditions unavailable, simulate with early-morning outdoor session + industrial fan for wind.
Duration 30 minutes standing (simulating corral wait). No vigorous movement. Arms occasionally raised (simulating stretching, checking phone, holding coffee).
Configurations tested Each tester wears 3 configurations in randomized order across 3 sessions (1 session per week, 3 weeks). Configs: A (single-layer met), B (met + nonwoven), C (met BOPLA + nonwoven). Optional: D (triple sandwich) if time allows.
Measurements — Objective Skin temperature at 4 sites (chest, back, forearm, thigh) using adhesive thermocouple sensors logged every 60 seconds. Ambient temperature + wind speed + humidity logged at start/end. FLIR thermal imaging at 0, 15, and 30 minutes.
Measurements — Subjective Thermal comfort scale (1–7 Likert: very cold to very warm) every 10 minutes. Overall comfort rating at end (1–10). “Would you buy this product?” (yes/no/maybe). Open-ended: “What would you improve?”
Tear-away test After 30-minute wear, each tester performs 3 tear-away attempts: (1) warm dry hands, (2) cold wet hands (simulating rain), (3) while walking briskly. Time each tear. Record: clean tear Y/N, seconds to remove, subjective ease (1–5).
Durability stress test After wear test: flex the poncho 50× at creases, check metallization delamination under 10× loupe. Spray with water bottle for 30 seconds, check leak-through. Pull-test seams with spring scale (target: >5 N/cm before tear).
Documentation Video all sessions (consent forms required). Photograph each config on each tester at 0 and 30 min. Compile data in spreadsheet. Statistical analysis: paired t-test comparing configs on each metric.
Decision criteria Winning config must: (1) maintain skin temp within 2°C of baseline for 30 min, (2) achieve ≥5/7 thermal comfort median, (3) tear away cleanly in ≤3 seconds in all 3 hand conditions, (4) receive >75% “would buy” responses, (5) cost ≤$1.50/unit at 100K volume.

8. CLO / Thermal Performance Testing Protocol

[GAP P2-06 CLOSED] CLO values (2.5–3.0) and the “95% infrared heat reflection” claim are cited throughout P0E, P1C, and P2A as the scientific foundation for warmth marketing and competitive equivalence vs. Heatsheets. However, these values have been estimated from published metallized film data, not measured on the actual assembled poncho. This section establishes the methodology for generating defensible thermal performance data.

8.1 What CLO Measures and Why It Matters

CLO is the standard unit of thermal insulation for clothing (1 CLO = 0.155 m²·K/W). A naked person at rest in still air at 21°C = 0 CLO. A typical business suit = 1.0 CLO. A heavy winter coat = 3–4 CLO. Heatsheets emergency blankets claim approximately 2.0–2.5 CLO equivalent based on IR reflectivity. Our target of 2.5–3.0 CLO positions the poncho as EQUAL TO OR BETTER THAN Heatsheets. The “95% IR reflection” figure comes from the physics of vacuum-metallized aluminum: aluminum reflects 95–97% of incident infrared radiation regardless of substrate. This is a material property claim (defensible). Converting it to CLO requires measuring the actual thermal resistance of the ASSEMBLED poncho, which depends on material layers, air gaps, body contact, and wind.

8.2 Testing Options: DIY to Lab-Grade

OPTION A — DIY Guarded Hot Plate (Budget: $200–$500): Build a simplified guarded hot plate apparatus. Components: electrically heated aluminum plate (12″×12″, maintained at 35°C / 95°F to simulate skin temperature), thermocouple sensors on heated surface and ambient, PID temperature controller ($50–80 on Amazon), insulation board (rigid foam, 2″ thick) surrounding the plate to prevent edge heat loss, and a power meter measuring watts required to maintain plate at 35°C. Protocol: (1) Run bare plate at 35°C in 20°C room; record steady-state power (Watts). (2) Place poncho sample over plate; record new steady-state power. (3) Thermal resistance R = A × ΔT / (Q_bare − Q_covered), where A = plate area, ΔT = plate-to-ambient temp difference. (4) CLO = R / 0.155. (5) Repeat 3× for each configuration; report mean ± SD. Accuracy: ±15–20% vs. lab-grade. Sufficient for directional comparison and marketing claims with qualification (“approximately X CLO in lab testing”).

OPTION B — University Textile Lab (Budget: $500–$2,000): Commission testing at an accredited textile lab using ASTM F1868 (Standard Test Method for Thermal and Evaporative Resistance of Clothing Materials Using a Sweating Hot Plate). Labs: Kansas State University Textile Testing Lab, NC State Wilson College of Textiles, University of Alberta Protective Clothing and Equipment Research Facility, or any ISO 11092 / ASTM F1868 accredited lab. Deliverable: formal test report with CLO value ± uncertainty, suitable for regulatory substantiation and marketing claims without qualification. Turnaround: 2–4 weeks. This is the RECOMMENDED option for generating defensible marketing data.

OPTION C — Thermal Manikin (Budget: $2,000–$30,000): Full-body thermal manikin testing per ASTM F1291 (Standard Test Method for Measuring the Thermal Insulation of Clothing Using a Heated Manikin). This is the gold standard for whole-garment thermal insulation measurement. A heated manikin shaped like a human body is dressed in the poncho and measures heat loss from 15–20 zones. Result: total body CLO. Available at: US Army Natick Soldier Center (government collaboration), Thermetrics (thermetrics.com, commercial testing), university labs with thermal manikin facilities. Appropriate for: investor presentations, academic publications, regulatory defense. Not necessary for V1 launch.

8.3 IR Reflectivity Measurement

The “95% IR reflection” claim can be substantiated separately from CLO using a simpler measurement. Method: use a FLIR ONE thermal camera ($200–$300) in a controlled protocol. (1) Heat a surface to 35°C (human skin temp). (2) Capture FLIR image of bare heated surface (shows high IR emission, surface appears bright). (3) Place poncho sample over surface, metallized side facing the surface. (4) Capture FLIR image of poncho exterior. (5) The temperature difference between bare surface and poncho exterior quantifies IR blocking. If exterior shows near-ambient temperature while surface underneath remains at 35°C, the metallization is reflecting >90% of IR radiation. This produces the FLIR comparison images needed for P1D Section 7 (FLIR thermal content) and the Amazon A+ Content comparison graphic (P1C Section 6.4 Module 2).

IMPORTANT: FLIR cameras measure SURFACE TEMPERATURE, not CLO directly. A FLIR image shows that the poncho is blocking IR radiation (which proves the metallization works), but converting FLIR temperature readings to CLO requires additional calculations involving emissivity, air gap, and convection. Use FLIR for marketing visuals (“thermal imaging shows heat retention”) and Option A/B for CLO numerical claims.

8.4 Comparative Testing: Poncho vs. Heatsheets vs. Garbage Bag

Using Option A (DIY hot plate) or Option B (university lab), test ALL THREE products under identical conditions: (1) Compostable poncho (Config B), (2) Heatsheets Classic blanket (purchase from shop.heatsheets.com, $150 for 100-count), (3) PE garbage bag (standard 13-gallon kitchen bag). Record CLO value for each. Expected results based on material physics: poncho CLO ≈ 2.5–3.0, Heatsheets ≈ 2.0–2.5 (thinner material, less air gap), garbage bag ≈ 0.3–0.5 (no IR reflection, minimal insulation). This comparative data becomes: (A) B2B pitch deck Slide 8 evidence (“Tested equivalent or better than Heatsheets”), (B) Amazon bullet point (“Lab-tested warmth comparable to leading finish-line blankets”), (C) race director objection handler (“Here are the test results”). Budget for comparative testing: $0 incremental if using the same hot plate or lab session.

9. Shelf Life Testing Protocol

Timepoint Tests Performed Storage Conditions
Baseline (0 months) Freshly manufactured samples; measure: IR reflectivity, tensile strength, tear resistance, visual appearance, metallization adhesion Store 20 samples each in: (A) climate-controlled 20°C/40% RH, (B) standard warehouse 25°C/50% RH, (C) accelerated aging 40°C/75% RH
6 months Pull 5 samples from each condition; repeat all baseline tests Compare to baseline; look for: haze/clouding on met surface, reduced tensile strength (>20% = fail), metallization delamination, brittleness
12 months Pull 5 samples from each condition; repeat all tests 12-month climate-controlled pass = minimum viable shelf life for seasonal product
18 months Pull 5 samples from each condition; final test If 18-month climate-controlled passes: excellent shelf life, enables year-round inventory. If fails: must produce within 12 months of use.
Accelerated aging correlation 40°C/75% RH for 3 months ≈ 12 months at 20°C/40% RH (Arrhenius approximation) Use accelerated aging for quick go/no-go on new material batches before committing to full production

SHELF LIFE IS THE #1 OPERATIONAL RISK for compostable products. PLA hydrolysis rate doubles for every 10°C increase. A summer warehouse at 35°C can degrade product in 3–6 months. Non-negotiable storage requirements: below 25°C (77°F), below 50% relative humidity, no direct UV exposure. Individual ponchos must be sealed in moisture-barrier packaging (the compostable inner bag specified in P1B Section 6). FIFO inventory management is essential.

Config C (ENVIROMET metallized BOPLA) has a natural shelf life advantage over Config B (NatureFlex cellulose) because the metallization layer on BOPLA is protective against moisture ingress to the PLA substrate. NatureFlex’s raw 60-day shelf life improves to 6–12 months when metallized and individually sealed — but Celplast’s DURAMET achieves 12–18+ months due to superior WVTR (0.93 g/m²/day). This is the primary argument for maintaining Config C as a dual-source option.

11. Supply Chain Risk Assessment & Contingency Plan

[GAP P2-04 CLOSED] The compostable film supply chain is concentrated among a small number of specialized manufacturers. A single-source dependency on any critical component creates an existential risk for a seasonal product with 8–10 week lead times: one supplier disruption can mean missing an entire race season. This section maps every single-source dependency, documents the backup for each, and establishes quantitative triggers for activating backup suppliers.

11.1 Single-Source Dependency Map

COMPONENT 1 — Metallized cellulose film (NatureFlex NM, Futamura): RISK LEVEL: HIGH. This is the core warmth-generating material. Futamura is the ONLY manufacturer of NatureFlex NM; no exact equivalent exists from any other supplier. Futamura operates two plants: Wigton, UK and Tecumseh, KS, USA. A disruption at either plant (fire, equipment failure, raw material shortage, labor dispute) could halt poncho production. Mitigation: Config C (Celplast ENVIROMET or TIPA 312MET) uses metallized BOPLA film, which provides equivalent warmth performance. Qualification status: evaluated in P2A Section 1 (scored 24/30 vs Config B’s 26/30). Switching cost: $500–$2,000 for converter retooling + $500–$1,500 for new sample validation. Switching time: 4–6 weeks for new samples + 4–6 weeks for production = 8–12 weeks total.

COMPONENT 2 — PLA spunbond nonwoven (35 gsm, Ahlstrom or China supplier): RISK LEVEL: MEDIUM. Multiple suppliers exist globally (Ahlstrom, Quanzhou Together Eco, NWFabric, Weboss, Spunindo, KINGFA). Single-source risk only if exclusive relationship with one supplier. Mitigation: maintain qualified relationships with 2+ nonwoven suppliers. PLA spunbond is a more commoditized product than metallized cellulose — substitution between suppliers requires minimal retooling (same material, similar specs). Switching time: 2–4 weeks for new samples + 2–4 weeks production = 4–8 weeks.

COMPONENT 3 — Natural rubber latex elastic (3mm cord): RISK LEVEL: LOW. Widely available commodity product with dozens of global suppliers. No single-source dependency. Switching time: 1–2 weeks.

COMPONENT 4 — Compostable ink (Sun Chemical or hubergroup): RISK LEVEL: LOW-MEDIUM. Two qualified ink systems identified. Both are large global chemical companies with multiple manufacturing facilities. Risk: specific COLOR formulation may have limited availability. Mitigation: maintain approved color specs with both suppliers. Switching time: 2–3 weeks.

COMPONENT 5 — Converting (die-cut, bonding, perforation, folding, packaging): RISK LEVEL: MEDIUM-HIGH. The converter relationship is the most operationally complex dependency: they hold the production tooling, know the machine settings, and produce the finished product. Losing a converter requires qualifying a new one from scratch. Mitigation: P2C Section 6 establishes a primary + backup converter from prototype phase. NEVER grant a converter exclusive manufacturing rights (per P2C Section 7 — Exclusivity & IP). Switching time: 6–12 weeks for full qualification of a new converter.

11.2 Backup Activation Triggers

Activate backup supplier if ANY of the following conditions are met: (1) PRIMARY LEAD TIME EXCEEDS 8 WEEKS — standard lead time is 4–6 weeks; if primary quotes >8 weeks for a standard order, initiate backup PO simultaneously. (2) PRICE INCREASE EXCEEDS 15% — compostable film prices fluctuate with corn/wood pulp commodity markets, but a >15% increase on a confirmed order signals supply stress or opportunistic pricing. Begin sourcing from backup. (3) QUALITY FAILURE ON 2 CONSECUTIVE BATCHES — one quality issue is correctable; two consecutive failures indicate a systemic process problem. Switch to backup while primary investigates and corrects. (4) FORCE MAJEURE EVENT — natural disaster, fire, pandemic disruption, or trade embargo affecting primary supplier’s production facility. Activate backup immediately. (5) CERTIFICATION LAPSE — if primary supplier’s BPI or EN 13432 certification expires or is revoked, product cannot ship. Switch to backup whose certs are current.

11.3 Warm Relationship Maintenance Protocol

The backup supplier must be kept “warm” — meaning they can produce poncho material within 4–6 weeks of activation, without re-qualification from scratch. Protocol: (1) Place a small ANNUAL ORDER with the backup film supplier (Celplast or TIPA): 500–1,000 m² ($125–$550 at evaluation pricing). This keeps the account active, the contact relationship warm, and generates a small batch of Config C material for shelf life testing and emergency stock. (2) Place a small annual order with the backup nonwoven supplier: 100–500 kg ($60–$250). (3) Maintain backup converter qualification: after the initial P2C prototype run from 2–3 converters, select the second-highest-scoring converter as backup. Place a small reorder every 12 months (500–1,000 units, $500–$1,500) to keep tooling current and operator familiarity fresh. (4) Update backup suppliers on any design changes (new dimensions, new perforation spec, new print artwork) within 30 days of change implementation at the primary supplier.

11.4 Safety Stock Policy

Hold a MINIMUM of 4–6 weeks of raw material safety stock for the most critical single-source component (metallized cellulose film). At 25,000 units/year run rate: annual film consumption = ~45,000 m². 6-week safety stock = ~5,200 m² of NatureFlex NM rolls. Cost: $1,300–$2,600 at volume pricing. Storage: climate-controlled per P2D Section 5 (≤25°C, ≤50% RH). FIFO rotation: use safety stock for production and replace with fresh rolls quarterly to prevent shelf-life degradation. Alternative: negotiate with Futamura to hold consignment stock at their US facility (Tecumseh, KS) — some suppliers offer this for committed customers.

FINISHED GOODS SAFETY STOCK: Hold 10% of confirmed B2B orders as buffer at the 3PL warehouse. Example: if 5,000 units ordered for Fall 2026 races, hold 500 extra units. Cost: $575–$900 (500 units at $1.15–$1.80 COGS). Purpose: covers short shipments, transit damage, and last-minute race director add-on orders. Replenish buffer after each race season.

11.5 Geopolitical & Trade Risk

If sourcing from China-based converters or nonwoven suppliers: monitor US-China trade policy for tariff changes affecting compostable packaging materials. Current HTS classification for compostable film ponchos: likely 3926.20 (articles of plastics for apparel) at 0–3.4% duty, or 6210.10 (garments of nonwoven fabric) at 2.8–8.0% duty. Section 301 tariffs (25% additional on selected Chinese goods) may apply — verify classification with a customs broker ($200–$500 for ruling). Mitigation: maintain a US/UK-based converter (Parkside, ProAmpac) as a tariff-free alternative even if China converter is lower cost. The 25% tariff differential can eliminate the China cost advantage overnight.

12.1 Recommended Decision: Config B (Primary) + Config C (Secondary)

PRIMARY: Metallized NatureFlex NM (23µm, Futamura) bonded via ultrasonic welding to PLA spunbond nonwoven (35 gsm, Ahlstrom or China supplier). Met side inward. Nonwoven outward. Target weight: 100–120g. Target CLO: 2.5–3.0. Target COGS: $1.15 at 100K. Certification: BPI + TÜV HOME (leveraging existing NatureFlex NM certs).

SECONDARY: Celplast ENVIROMET (25µm) or TIPA 312MET bonded to PLA spunbond (35 gsm). Same orientation. Target weight: 90–115g. Target CLO: 2.4–2.8. Target COGS: $1.05–$1.35 at 100K. DECISION GATE: The field trial (Section 5) is the decisive test. If Config B scores ≥24/30 on the evaluation matrix AND >75% “would buy,” proceed with Config B. If NEITHER config achieves >75% “would buy,” return to material R&D and test Config D (triple sandwich) before committing to production.

14. Conclusion: Material Selection Is the Product

For a single-product brand, the material IS the product. Every claim in P1C (95% heat reflection, certified compostable, composts in 90 days) depends on the material performing exactly as specified. Every negative review stems from material failure (tears, doesn’t keep warm, feels cheap). Every race director reorder depends on the poncho surviving 30–90 minutes on a runner in cold, wet, windy conditions.

The field trial is non-negotiable. No amount of supplier data sheets or lab reports substitutes for 8+ real runners wearing the poncho at 6 AM in 38°F weather and telling you whether they’d spend $9.99 on it. Run the trial. Trust the data. Let the runners decide.

Config B (NatureFlex NM + PLA spunbond) is the current frontrunner based on P0E research synthesis, but the field trial in Weeks 6–7 is the binding decision point. If the data says Config C wins, follow the data. If neither config achieves 75% “would buy,” pause and invest in Config D before committing $50K+ to production inventory. The worst outcome is launching with a material that fails in the corral. The second-worst outcome is delaying launch by 4 weeks to get the material right. The math is clear.
