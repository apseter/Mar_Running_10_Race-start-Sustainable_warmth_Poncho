Compostable vs. Biodegradable: What Runners Should Know

Target keyword: "compostable vs biodegradable" · ~1,800 words · Environmental Claims (P9C)

You see both words on product labels. You assume they mean the same thing. They don't — and the difference matters more than you think.

"Biodegradable" means: the material will eventually break down through natural biological processes. Eventually. That could be 12 weeks. Or 500 years. The word has no legally defined timeframe. A LDPE garbage bag is technically "biodegradable" — it just takes five centuries.

"Compostable" means: the material breaks down into usable compost (soil-enriching humus) within a specific timeframe under specific conditions, leaving no toxic residue. The timeframe and conditions are defined by international standards — ASTM D6400 in the US, EN 13432 in Europe.

The FTC's Position

The Federal Trade Commission's Green Guides — the regulatory framework for environmental marketing claims in the US — draws a hard line on both terms.

On "biodegradable": the FTC says you shouldn't claim a product is biodegradable unless the entire product will decompose within "a reasonably short period" after customary disposal. The FTC considers "reasonably short" to be one year or less. A PLA-based product in a landfill? 100+ years. That's not "reasonably short." The FTC has fined companies $100K–$1M+ for unsubstantiated biodegradable claims.

On "compostable": the FTC says you can claim a product is compostable if it breaks down in a safe and timely manner in an appropriate composting facility. But — and this is critical — if the product only composts in a commercial facility (not in a backyard compost bin), the claim must be qualified: "compostable in commercial composting facilities."

An unqualified "compostable" on your product label implies it composts anywhere, including a home backyard pile. If it doesn't — and most certified compostable products don't — that unqualified claim is deceptive under FTC rules.

What BPI Certification Actually Tests

BPI (Biodegradable Products Institute) certification is the gold standard for compostability claims in the US market. When a product carries the BPI logo, it means the product has passed three rigorous laboratory tests under ASTM D6400:

  1. Biodegradation: at least 60% of the product's organic carbon must convert to CO₂ within 180 days at 58°C (136°F) — the temperature maintained inside a commercial composting facility
  2. Disintegration: at least 90% of the product must physically break apart and pass through a 2mm sieve after 84 days of composting
  3. Ecotoxicity: the resulting compost must support plant growth at 90% or better compared to control compost — proving the material doesn't leave toxic residue in the soil

These tests take 6–9 months to complete. They cost $5,000–$15,000. They require an accredited laboratory (OWS, RISE, or TUV Austria). And they must be conducted on the finished assembled product — not just the raw materials.

This last point is frequently overlooked. A NatureFlex film may have its own compostability certificate. A PLA spunbond may have its own certificate. But the assembled poncho — with adhesive, ink, and all components bonded together — must be independently tested as a whole. The adhesive alone could compromise compostability. BPI certification confirms the entire product, not just its ingredients.

Why "Biodegradable" Is the #1 Greenwashing Trigger

The word "biodegradable" has become so overused and so misleading that it's essentially a regulatory red flag. Here's why:

  • Every material on Earth is technically biodegradable given enough time — including steel, glass, and concrete. The word communicates nothing specific.
  • Companies use it to imply environmental benefit without substantiation. "Biodegradable garbage bags" are still LDPE that takes 500+ years in a landfill. The claim is technically true and functionally meaningless.
  • The FTC has taken enforcement action against multiple companies for unsubstantiated biodegradable claims, including penalties exceeding $1 million.
  • California, Washington, Oregon, and Colorado have enacted state-level labeling laws that restrict or require qualification of biodegradable claims.

For runners evaluating race-day products: if a product says "biodegradable" without specifying conditions, timeframe, and certification — treat the claim with skepticism. If it says "compostable" without "at commercial composting facilities" — ask why the qualification is missing.

What This Means for Your Next Race

If you're a runner who cares about the environmental impact of your race participation, here's the practical guidance:

  1. Skip the garbage bag. It's free, it works for 30 minutes, and it will outlast you by 499 years.
  2. Look for BPI certification. The BPI logo on a product means it has been independently tested and verified to compost under commercial conditions. No logo, no verification.
  3. Check for the qualification. "Compostable at commercial composting facilities" is honest. "Compostable" without qualification may be misleading.
  4. Ask your race director. If your race distributes Mylar blankets or garbage bags at the start line, ask: "Is there a compostable option?" Race directors respond to runner demand.
