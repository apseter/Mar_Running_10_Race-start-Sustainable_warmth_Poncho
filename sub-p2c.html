Executive Summary

P2C is where the product becomes REAL. P2A selected the materials (Config B: NatureFlex NM + PLA spunbond 35gsm). P2B designed the product (55″×34″, elastic hood, center-front tear-away, 4 branding zones). P2C orders 50–100 prototype units from 2–3 converters, subjects them to a rigorous 10-person corral simulation test, validates the tear-away system in 7 conditions, tests co-brand printing quality, evaluates fit across XS–XXL body types, selects the winning supplier, and negotiates final production terms.

The critical decision at the end of P2C: commit to a production order of 5,000–25,000 units (Moderate scenario from P0F) or iterate on the prototype. This decision is gated by the corral simulation results: ≥8/10 testers must rate warmth ≥6/10, achieve clean tear-away in ≤3 seconds with cold wet hands, and say “yes” to paying $9.99. If these thresholds are not met, do NOT proceed to production — iterate until they are.

1.2 Prototype Receiving Inspection

When prototypes arrive, conduct IMMEDIATE inspection before scheduling corral simulation. Checklist: (1) Count units (full quantity received?). (2) Measure 5 random units against P2B dimensional specs (±0.5″ tolerance). (3) Visual inspection: metallization quality, bonding integrity, perforation visibility, print quality. (4) Quick tear test: 3 units torn at room temperature — clean path? Reasonable force? (5) Weight check: 3 units on kitchen scale (±10% of 100–120g target). (6) Fold/pack test: can 3 units be refolded into 6″×4.5″ inner bag? Document ALL findings with photographs. If any converter’s prototypes fail basic inspection, do NOT include them in the corral simulation — request revised samples.

2. 10-Person Corral Simulation Test

This is the most important test in the entire product development process. It answers the ONE question that matters: will a real runner, standing in a cold corral at 6 AM, choose this product over a garbage bag and tell their friends about it? P2A’s 8-tester field trial validated MATERIALS. P2C’s 10-person simulation validates the COMPLETE PRODUCT from a converter.

Element Protocol
Testers 10 runners minimum (8 from P2A field trial + 2 fresh testers for unbiased perspective). Mix: 3 female, 3 male, 2 non-binary or additional; sizes XS, S, M, L, XL, XXL represented. At least 2 first-time marathon runners.
Location Outdoor venue accessible at 5:30–7:30 AM: park, parking lot, school track, or local 5K race start area. Must be open to wind. Ideal: attend an actual local race and conduct test with consenting participants.
Conditions target 35–45°F (2–7°C), wind 5–20 mph, overcast or light drizzle. If unavailable: earliest cold morning available + industrial fan for wind simulation. Log: ambient temp (digital thermometer), wind speed (handheld anemometer $15–20), humidity (hygrometer), precipitation.
Pre-test setup Each tester receives: converter-produced prototype (V0.3), adhesive thermocouple sensors (4 sites: chest, upper back, forearm, thigh), FLIR thermal baseline capture. Tester wears their normal race-day base layer under the poncho.
Phase 1: Standing (0–30 min) Testers stand in a simulated corral. Permitted: stretching, checking phone, holding coffee cup, chatting — all normal corral activities. NOT permitted: vigorous movement, jogging. Record: skin temperature every 5 min, thermal comfort (1–7 Likert) every 10 min.
Phase 2: Walking (30–35 min) Testers walk 400m at a brisk pace (simulating walk to start corral). Assess: does poncho shift? Does hood stay on? Do side slits allow arm swing? Does hemline interfere with stride?
Phase 3: Tear-away (35–40 min) Simulated gun: testers tear off poncho using pull tab. 3 attempts each: (1) warm dry hands, (2) cold wet hands (spray with water + wait 2 min in cold), (3) while walking briskly. Time each tear. Photograph tear path. Record: clean tear Y/N, seconds, ease rating (1–5).
Phase 4: Debrief (40–60 min) Individual 10-minute interviews: warmth rating (1–10), overall comfort (1–10), “Would you pay $9.99 for this?” (yes/no/maybe + reasoning), “What would you improve?” (open-ended), “Would you recommend to a running friend?” (NPS-style 0–10).
Documentation Video all phases (consent forms signed). FLIR captures at 0, 15, 30 min. Photograph: each tester front + back at 0 and 30 min. Photograph: every tear path. All data into a master spreadsheet within 24 hours.
Success criteria ≥8/10 testers rate warmth ≥6/10. ≥8/10 rate comfort ≥6/10. ≥8/10 achieve clean tear in ≤3 sec in ALL 3 hand conditions. ≥8/10 say “yes” to $9.99. Mean NPS ≥50. ZERO safety concerns (e.g., choking hazard from elastic, sharp edges from perforation).

Source testers from: local running clubs (Fleet Feet group runs, November Project), r/running local city threads, Strava Club members, personal running network. 2.3 Comparative Testing

For maximum data value, include COMPARISON products in the simulation. Each tester also wears (in randomized order across separate sessions or sequential 15-min blocks): (A) a standard PE garbage bag, (B) a Heatsheets Classic blanket (if available; order $150 100-count from shop.heatsheets.com), (C) a thrift-store sweatshirt. At end, each tester ranks all 4 options on: warmth, comfort, convenience, willingness to pay. This comparative data is GOLD for marketing (“9 out of 10 runners preferred [BRAND] over a garbage bag”) and for the B2B pitch deck (P1D Slide 8).

3. Tear-Away Validation: 7-Condition Testing Matrix

The tear-away system was designed in P2B with target parameters (3–5 N initiation, 2mm/1mm perforation). P2C validates those parameters on CONVERTER-PRODUCED prototypes, which may behave differently from hand-fabricated samples due to: machine perforation consistency, bonding method variations, material batch differences, and aging during shipping.

Condition Setup Protocol Pass Criteria Purpose
Warm dry hands (baseline) Room temp; hands dry 10 tears per tester; record time + clean Y/N Expected: 100% clean, <2 sec avg Validates basic perforation spec
Cold dry hands Hands exposed to 35°F for 5 min; NOT wet 10 tears per tester Expected: 100% clean, <3 sec avg Fine motor control reduced but grip intact
Cold wet hands (critical) Hands sprayed with water then exposed to 35°F for 3 min 10 tears per tester MUST achieve: ≥90% clean, ≤3 sec avg THE definitive test. If this fails, redesign pull tab.
Gloved hands Tester wears thin running gloves (liner weight) 10 tears per tester Expected: ≥80% clean, <4 sec avg Many runners wear gloves at start; pull tab must be findable through gloves
While walking briskly Tester walks at 4 mph and tears without stopping 5 tears per tester Expected: ≥80% clean, <4 sec avg Simulates runners who tear off while already moving after gun
One-handed (emergency) Tester tears with dominant hand only; other hand holds phone/coffee 5 tears per tester Expected: ≥70% clean, <5 sec avg Edge case but realistic: runners often hold items at start
Wind loading pre-test Poncho worn in 15–20 mph wind for 10 min; then tear 3 tears per tester Poncho must NOT have torn at perforations during 10-min wind exposure Confirms perforation survives sustained wind before intentional tear

3.1 Data Collection & Analysis

Record for EVERY tear attempt: tester ID, condition, converter (A/B/C), time to complete removal (stopwatch, 0.1-sec precision), clean tear path (Y/N, photograph), subjective ease rating (1–5), and any failure mode observed. Minimum: 10 testers × 7 conditions × varying reps = 350–500 individual tear data points. Analyze: mean tear time per condition, clean-tear rate per condition, and pass/fail against P2B criteria. If ANY condition falls below its pass threshold: document the failure mode, propose a specific engineering fix (enlarge pull tab, change perforation spacing, add grip texture), and request revised prototypes from the converter before production commitment.

3.2 Statistical Analysis Methodology

[GAP P2-15 CLOSED] With 10 testers, statistical power is limited but sufficient for large effect sizes. Pre-registering the analysis plan before data collection prevents post-hoc rationalization and strengthens credibility with investors and B2B customers who may challenge rigor. The following methods apply to ALL quantitative data collected in P2C.

CONTINUOUS MEASURES (skin temperature, tear time, thermal comfort rating, overall comfort rating): Use PAIRED t-test when comparing two configurations on the same tester (e.g., Config B warmth vs. Config C warmth for each tester). Use repeated-measures ANOVA when comparing 3+ configurations simultaneously. Report: mean ± standard deviation per configuration, p-value (significance threshold α = 0.05), and 95% confidence interval for the difference. If data is non-normally distributed (check with Shapiro-Wilk test): use Wilcoxon signed-rank test (paired) or Friedman test (3+ conditions) instead of parametric tests. Tool: Google Sheets (T.TEST function) or free online calculator (socscistatistics.com).

CATEGORICAL OUTCOMES (clean tear Y/N, would-buy Y/N): Use McNemar’s test for paired binary comparisons (same tester, two conditions) or Cochran’s Q test for 3+ conditions. For simple proportion comparison (e.g., “90% achieved clean tear in cold-wet condition”): report proportion + exact binomial 95% confidence interval. With N=10: a 9/10 success rate has a 95% CI of [55.5%, 99.7%] — wide but directionally useful. A 10/10 success rate has a 95% CI of [69.2%, 100%]. Tool: VassarStats.net (free online binomial CI calculator).

ORDINAL RATINGS (Likert 1–7 thermal comfort, 1–10 overall comfort, 0–10 NPS): Use Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired comparisons. Report: median and interquartile range (IQR) per configuration (NOT mean — Likert data is ordinal, not interval). For NPS: calculate Promoters (9–10), Passives (7–8), Detractors (0–6). NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors. With N=10: NPS has wide confidence intervals but provides a directional benchmark.

SAMPLE SIZE JUSTIFICATION: 10 testers × 3 configurations = 30 paired observations. Statistical power for a paired t-test with N=10 at α=0.05: detects large effects (Cohen’s d ≥ 0.8) with ~80% power, detects medium effects (d ≈ 0.5) with ~40% power, cannot reliably detect small effects (d < 0.3). PRACTICAL IMPLICATION: this sample size is sufficient to detect meaningful differences between configurations (e.g., Config B is noticeably warmer than Config C) but cannot detect subtle differences (e.g., 0.5°C mean temperature difference). This limitation should be acknowledged in the prototype evaluation report. If resources allow: increase to 15–20 testers for the final P2C simulation (10 from P2A + 5–10 new recruits) to improve power.

PRE-REGISTRATION: Document the analysis plan (which tests, which comparisons, what significance threshold) BEFORE collecting data. Store the plan as a dated, signed document (PDF or Google Doc with version history). This prevents the temptation to run multiple analyses after the fact and report only favorable results. Pre-registration is standard practice in clinical trials and increasingly expected in consumer product research.

3.3 Converter Comparison

Run identical tear-away tests on prototypes from each converter. Compare: (A) mean tear force (measured with spring gauge — $15–30 on Amazon), (B) clean-tear percentage, (C) perforation consistency (examine 10 units under 10× loupe: is cut/bridge ratio consistent within ±0.2mm?). The converter with the most consistent, clean perforations within the 3–5 N target range scores highest on this criterion.

4. Co-Brand Printing Quality Validation

Co-branded ponchos are the primary B2B revenue driver. If the print quality is poor (faded, misaligned, or rubs off during wear), race directors will not reorder and the sponsor co-branding model collapses. P2C tests printing on actual converter-produced prototypes — not just material swatches — because printing on a pre-assembled, bonded, perforated poncho introduces variables that swatch testing misses.

Test Method Acceptance Criteria Standard Reference
Print adhesion — tape test Apply 3M Scotch tape firmly to printed area; peel at 180° angle. Examine: ink transfer to tape (should be <5% area). Test on 5 units from each converter. If >5% ink lifts: primer coat required on nonwoven surface before printing. ASTM D3359 cross-hatch adhesion test (simplified field version)
Print durability — body heat Wear printed poncho for 30 min at body temperature; examine print area for: smearing, fading, ink transfer to base layer underneath. Test on all 10 corral simulation testers’ ponchos post-test. Photograph print zones before and after wear. Simulate: body heat (98.6°F) + moisture (condensation) + movement (flexing at printed zones)
Print durability — rain Spray printed area with water bottle for 60 sec; wipe with paper towel. Examine: ink bleeding, smearing, or removal. Test 3 units per converter. If ink bleeds: switch to UV-resistant compostable ink (hubergroup outdoor series). Drizzle resistance of print, not full waterproofing
Print legibility — distance View printed back panel from 10 ft, 25 ft, and 50 ft. Can race logo + sponsor logo be identified at each distance? Test with 5 people with normal vision. At 25 ft, logo must be identifiable. At 50 ft, brand/color must be recognizable. Critical for co-brand value: sponsors pay for visibility
Co-brand mockup accuracy Compare actual printed prototype against the Canva/Figma co-brand template mockup from P1B Section 7. Overlay photographs digitally. Color match: ΔE <5 (acceptable for flexo on nonwoven). Size: ±0.5″ of template spec. Race directors will compare the mockup they approved against the actual product. Must match closely.
Multi-color registration For co-brand prints requiring 2+ colors: check registration alignment between colors. Acceptable: ±1mm misalignment. Examine 10 printed units with 10× loupe. If >2 units exceed ±1mm: adjust print cylinder alignment at converter. Single-color (white on nonwoven) avoids registration issues entirely; multi-color for premium co-brands only

4.1 Print Method Comparison

Request from each converter: 10 units with single-color flexographic print (brand logo on back panel, white ink on nonwoven surface). AND 5 units with 2-color screen print (simulated co-brand: brand logo + placeholder race logo in contrasting colors). Compare: ink coverage consistency, edge sharpness (halftone dots visible at 12″?), registration accuracy between colors, and cost per unit difference between methods.

EXPECTED OUTCOME: Flexo printing on PLA spunbond nonwoven produces acceptable results for 1–2 color applications. Screen printing offers slightly better ink opacity and color accuracy but at higher per-unit cost (+$0.02–$0.05/unit). For standard (non-co-branded) ponchos, flexo is sufficient. For premium co-branded ponchos, screen printing may be worth the premium — test and decide based on converter samples.

5. Fit Testing Across Body Types: XS to XXL+

The corral simulation (Section 2) tests the poncho on 10 runners across multiple body types. This section provides the specific fit evaluation framework to ensure the poncho serves the widest possible range of runners while being honest about its limitations.

Size Range Expected Fit Notes / Action Items
XS/S (chest 32–38″, height 5’0″–5’5″) Poncho fits loosely with generous drape. Hem reaches knee or below knee. Hood is proportionally large but functional with elastic adjustment. May feel oversized. Document: “Generously cut for layering.” Hem length at knee level is acceptable for standing; would be long for running but the poncho is removed before running.
M (chest 38–42″, height 5’5″–5’10″) Optimal fit. Hem at mid-thigh. Hood frames face with elastic adjusted. Arms move freely through oversized armholes. This is the “design center” body type. Product photos should feature this size range.
L (chest 42–46″, height 5’10″–6’1″) Good fit. Hem at upper thigh. Width comfortable with hydration vest underneath. Hood fits over baseball cap. Second priority for product photography. Validate that branding zones remain centered on back panel.
XL (chest 46–50″, height 6’0″–6’3″) Functional but snug through torso. Hem at high thigh. Armholes adequate but less excess fabric. Test: does snug fit cause discomfort during 30-min standing? Does metallized film crinkle/noise increase at tighter fit?
XXL+ (chest 50–54″+, height varies) At the limit of “one size fits most.” Width is tight; may pull across chest. Length is short on taller individuals. DOCUMENT HONESTLY: at 54″+ chest, fit is tight but poncho is wearable. At 56″+ chest, consider: the poncho may not close fully at sides. Note in product listing. Evaluate XL variant for V1.5.
Arms/mobility test (all sizes) Can tester: raise arms overhead? Reach into side slits for race bib? Hold coffee cup? Check phone? Stretch quads? All sizes must pass the “5 movement test.” If ANY size fails ANY movement: adjust armhole dimensions or side slit length.

5.1 Fit Data Documentation

For each tester, record: height, weight, chest circumference (over race-day base layer), and self-reported T-shirt size. Photograph front + back + side at 0 and 30 minutes. Document: hemline position (knee/mid-thigh/upper-thigh/hip), armhole gap (tight/comfortable/loose), hood coverage (full/partial/falls back), and side slit accessibility. Rate overall fit: 1 (too small) — 3 (perfect) — 5 (too large).

5.2 Sizing Decision for V1

V1 launches as “one size fits most” with honest sizing documentation per P1B Section 11.5. If fit testing reveals that ≥20% of the running population (e.g., chest >50″ or height <5’0″) experiences poor fit, evaluate: (A) adjusting base dimensions for V1 (wider or longer), OR (B) planning a second size (S/M and L/XL) for V1.5. Two sizes doubles inventory complexity and COGS per SKU — avoid if possible for V1.

6.1 Scoring Process

Score each sub-factor 1–5 (1 = poor, 5 = excellent). Multiply sub-factor average by category weight. Sum weighted scores for each converter. Maximum possible: 500 (all 5s × 100% weight). MINIMUM viable score for production supplier: 350/500. If no converter reaches 350: request revised samples, renegotiate terms, or source additional converters before committing.

DECISION: Select the highest-scoring converter as PRIMARY supplier. Second-highest becomes BACKUP supplier (maintain relationship with small reorder every 6–12 months). Document the scoring rationale in writing — this becomes the basis for future supplier audits and any switching decisions.

6.2 Supplier Site Visit (If Budget Allows)

If the primary supplier is domestic (US/UK): schedule a 1-day factory visit ($200–$500 travel cost). Observe: production line cleanliness, material handling, QC processes, equipment condition, and workforce experience with compostable materials. If the primary supplier is in China: consider a third-party inspection service (QIMA $300–$500 per inspection, AsiaInspection $250–$450) for a pre-production audit. This is NOT mandatory for a prototype run but HIGHLY recommended before committing to a 25K+ unit production order.

7. Production Terms Negotiation

Negotiate production terms BEFORE placing the first bulk PO. Everything is negotiable at this stage — the converter wants your business and will make concessions to secure a multi-year customer. The following terms should be formalized in a written Manufacturing Agreement or Purchase Order Terms document.

7.1 Manufacturing Agreement Template

Have a business attorney ($500–$1,500) draft a Manufacturing Agreement covering: (1) Pricing and payment terms, (2) MOQ and lead time commitments, (3) Quality standards and defect definitions, (4) IP ownership and NDA, (5) Exclusivity limitations, (6) Force majeure clause, (7) Dispute resolution (specify jurisdiction), (8) Term and termination conditions. For a first order of 5K–25K units, a detailed Purchase Order with attached Terms & Conditions is sufficient; a full Manufacturing Agreement becomes necessary at 50K+ annual volume or when adding co-brand production.

7.2 First Production Order Decision Gate

DO NOT place the first production order until ALL of the following are confirmed: (1) Corral simulation ≥8/10 pass rate on warmth + comfort + tear-away + buy intent. (2) Tear-away validation passes in ALL 7 conditions (Section 3). (3) Print quality meets acceptance criteria (Section 4). (4) Fit testing confirms “one size fits most” works for ≥80% of body types. (5) Pricing terms are within P0F target ranges. (6) BPI certification is initiated or supplier certifications are leverageable for soft launch. (7) At least 3 race director LOIs confirmed for Fall 2026 pilots (from P1E B2B outreach). If ANY gate fails: document the issue, propose a fix, and re-test before committing inventory dollars.

8. Timeline: 5-Week Prototype Sprint

WEEK 8: Send factory tech spec to 2–3 converters. Pay sample deposits. Receive and inspect any fast-turnaround prototypes (ProAmpac may deliver in 2 weeks). Begin preparing corral simulation logistics (venue, testers, equipment).

9. Prototype-to-Production Readiness Checklist

✅ Corral simulation: ≥8/10 testers pass warmth (≥6/10) + comfort (≥6/10) + buy intent ($9.99 yes) + NPS (≥50)

10. Conclusion: The Prototype Is the Product’s Audition

The prototype phase is the product’s audition in front of its future customers. 10 runners standing in the cold for 30 minutes will tell you more about the product’s viability than any spreadsheet, material data sheet, or competitor analysis. Their skin temperature data quantifies warmth. Their tear-away times quantify convenience. Their “would you pay $9.99?” answers quantify market demand. Their open-ended improvement suggestions write the V1.5 roadmap.

The comparison test is the hidden weapon. When 9 out of 10 testers prefer the compostable poncho over a garbage bag, that data point becomes the lead of every sales email, the hero stat of the pitch deck, and the first line of every Amazon review response. “In independent testing, 90% of runners preferred [BRAND] over their current start-line solution.” That sentence is worth the entire P2C budget.

Do not skip. Do not rush. Do not cut corners. A single weekend of rigorous prototype testing prevents months of customer complaints, negative reviews, and race-director disappointment. The product’s reputation is set by its first 100 users. Make those 100 users the 10 testers who helped design it and the 90 runners at your first 3 pilot races. They become your advocates, your reviewers, and your referral network. Give them a product worth advocating for.
