Executive Summary

Eight distinct B2B channels can deliver the compostable warmth poncho to race directors and runners. Registration add-ons via RunSignUp (50%+ US market share) offer the highest margin at 56–60% net with near-zero fixed costs. Fleet Feet’s vendor partner program (280+ stores, 19 brands, history of launching HOKA and On) provides specialty retail credibility. Marathon expos deliver brand awareness and race-director networking despite thin direct margins. Sustainability certifications (CRS, Racing for Sustainability) serve as powerful B2B selling tools — the poncho directly earns credit under CRS waste management criteria.

2. Race Supply Distribution: How Products Actually Reach Races

2.1 The Heatsheets Direct-Sales Model

Heatsheets (Advanced Flexible Materials) sells primarily direct to race organizers via email and phone relationships built over 45+ years. Their webshop (shop.heatsheets.com) serves smaller orders; bulk orders (15+ cartons / 3,000+ blankets) require emailing info@heatsheets.com for tiered pricing. Custom-printed blankets require 3,000-unit MOQ. All products are produced, printed, and converted in North America. Free shipping on orders under 120 lbs to US addresses.

Key insight: Heatsheets’ model is relationship-driven with high switching costs. Race directors reorder annually from established contacts. Breaking in requires offering something Heatsheets cannot: compostability + CRS certification credit + sponsor sustainability narrative.

2.2 MDS Associates: Medical/Safety Distributor

MDS Associates (mdsassociates.com) is a wholesale safety and medical supply distributor that resells generic mylar/foil blankets (Dynarex brand) and other emergency supplies to races, EMS, and event organizers. They do NOT carry Heatsheets. Their role: serving small/mid-size races that buy generic blankets at $0.60–$0.81/unit rather than premium Heatsheets at $1.43+. MDS operates on standard distributor margins of 15–25%.

2.3 Universal Marathon Blankets (UMB)

Universal Packaging (direct.univpack.com) sells pre-cut 48″×72″ marathon blankets in bulk at $0.60–$0.70/unit (250-count rolls). They are the primary budget alternative to Heatsheets, used by cost-conscious races that prioritize price over branding. UMB blankets are NOT recyclable through the Heatsheets/Trex partnership — they end up in landfill.

2.4 Small vs. Large Race Procurement

Small races (<1,000 runners): single race director makes all purchasing decisions; buys via Amazon Business, local medical supply, or direct from UMB. Budget: $5K–$10K total. Mid-size (1,000–5,000): race director + small committee; mix of direct purchases and distributor relationships. Large (10,000+): dedicated operations staff; long-term vendor contracts; 6–12 month procurement cycles; Heatsheets-style direct relationships.

3.1 RunSignUp Add-On Technical Setup

RunSignUp offers three merchandise systems: Giveaways (free, one per event), Add-Ons (optional during/after registration, registrants only), and Store (standalone e-commerce for anyone). A third-party vendor CANNOT auto-list across multiple races — each race director must individually configure the add-on in their dashboard under Race > Giveaways/Add-Ons > Registration Add Ons.

Practical implication: You must sell the concept to each race director individually, then provide them with product images, descriptions, and pricing to configure in their RunSignUp dashboard. This is a relationship sale, not a platform listing. The vendor receives purchase data through the race director’s participant reports (exportable to CSV, Google Sheets, Excel).

3.2 Fulfillment: Packet Pickup vs. Direct Ship

Two models: (A) Bulk-ship to race venue for inclusion in bib packet pickup — $1.00–$1.20/unit fulfillment, requires coordination with race logistics team. (B) Direct-ship to individual runners — $4.00–$5.00/unit shipping, margin-destructive. Model A is strongly preferred for races with mandatory in-person packet pickup (most majors). Model B works for virtual add-on purchases at races with mail-out options.

3.3 ACTIVE.com Vendor Portal Advantage

ACTIVE.com’s vendor portal offers a more automated integration than RunSignUp. Third-party vendors log into their own portal to check sales and fulfill orders, with automatic revenue sharing. This makes ACTIVE the more natural platform partner for scaling across many races. However, RunSignUp’s 50%+ market share makes it the volume priority.

The TCS NYC Marathon Health & Fitness Expo at Javits Center runs 3 days before race day with 100,000+ total visitors. Primary goal at expos is NOT direct profit — it’s brand awareness, runner feedback, email list capture, and race-director networking. Staff booth with 2–3 people who are runners themselves. Offer $8.99 single / $14.99 2-pack impulse pricing. Collect emails with “get 15% off your next order” sign-up. Have a separate “Race Directors” area with B2B materials, sample kits, and pricing sheets.

6.1 Overcoming the Heatsheets Switching Cost

The pitch is NOT “replace Heatsheets.” Heatsheets owns the finish line. The pitch is: “We own the start line — a moment Heatsheets doesn’t serve.” This positions the poncho as complementary, not competitive, avoiding the switching-cost objection entirely. For races that DO use Heatsheets ponchos at the start: lead with sustainability certification credit (CRS/RFS), sponsor co-branding opportunity, and operational simplification (compostable = no sorting needed).

6.2 Common Objections and Responses

"Too expensive vs. garbage bags/mylar." Response: Total cost analysis — factor in 200+ clothing-sorting volunteers, Goodwill coordination, and brand/sponsor value of a co-branded compostable product.

"Will it actually keep runners warm?" Response: Metallized aluminum reflects 95–97% IR body heat — identical physics to Heatsheets. Provide thermal test data (CLO comparison).

"Is it really compostable?" Response: BPI certified (#pending), EN 13432 compliant, TÜV OK Compost verified. Third-party lab results available.

"We already have a sustainability program." Response: This poncho directly earns CRS certification credit under waste management criteria. Show exactly which CRS checklist items it satisfies.

7.1 CRS Certification Credit: Specific Criteria Satisfied

The Council for Responsible Sport evaluates races across 5 pillars with 61 best practices. A compostable poncho directly satisfies: (1) Waste Management — Waste Reduction: offering compostable alternatives to disposable products. (2) Waste Management — Diversion: achieving higher compost/recycling rates by replacing non-recyclable mylar. (3) Procurement — Sustainable Sourcing: purchasing certified compostable/bio-based products. (4) Innovation: introducing novel sustainability solutions. Each satisfied criterion earns points toward higher certification tiers.

7.2 Racing for Sustainability Industry Partnership

Racing for Sustainability (Tina Muir) offers Industry Partner membership at $1,000/year. Benefits: toolkit inclusion (recommended products list), digital badge, promotion to member races, conference speaking opportunities. 78% of races are too small for CRS — RFS fills this gap. The compostable poncho becomes a “recommended product” in the RFS toolkit, driving organic B2B leads.

8.1 How the Heatsheets Co-Branding Model Works

Heatsheets’ core business model: race directors sell blanket ad space to sponsors. Sponsor pays for custom-printed blankets ($2–$5/unit at volume) with their logo prominently displayed. Runner receives a useful product; sponsor gets guaranteed impressions (every finisher uses the blanket); race director offsets costs. Custom print MOQ: 3,000 units. This model translates directly to a compostable poncho: sponsor pays $3–$5/unit for co-branded compostable poncho + sustainability PR story.

8.2 Approaching Race Sponsors

The pitch to sponsors: “Your logo on the only compostable warmth product at the start line — 60,000+ runners wearing your brand for 30–90 minutes in front of cameras, with a sustainability story that generates earned media.” Target sponsors with ESG mandates: Bank of America (requires compostable at Chicago), Schneider Electric (Boston sustainability partner), TCS ($40M/yr across 5 Majors with sustainability as stated pillar). Typical activation budget: $50K–$250K for a World Marathon Major sponsorship package.

9. Charity & Nonprofit Race Partnerships

Charity races represent ~15–20% of all US running events. Major charity running programs include Team in Training (Leukemia & Lymphoma Society — raised $1.5B+ since 1988), AIDS Walk/Run events, Susan G. Komen Race for the Cure (largest series of 5K runs globally), and St. Jude Heroes marathon program. Charity race budgets are typically 10–20% tighter than for-profit races, with higher scrutiny on per-runner costs.

However, charity races are MORE likely to invest in sustainability because (1) their participants skew toward socially conscious demographics, (2) sustainability aligns with mission-driven branding, and (3) environmental claims help attract corporate sponsors. Heatsheets explicitly markets to nonprofits with a dedicated page showing how custom-printed blankets can be part of fundraising — a model to emulate.

Group purchasing: no formal buying cooperative exists for US race supplies. However, race series operators (REVEL, Rock ’n’ Roll/Ironman, Spartan) centralize procurement across multiple events — one conversation can unlock an entire portfolio. REVEL series (7+ races at cold mountain starts) is the single best series target.

10.1 Certification Requirements by Market

EU/UK: EN 13432 mandatory; TÜV OK Compost preferred. US certifications (ASTM D6400, BPI) are NOT recognized. Japan: Japanese Green Purchasing Law + JIS standards. Australia: AS 4736 (industrial) / AS 5810 (home compostable). Dual US + EU certification ($35K–$75K, 12–18 months) covers the two largest marathon markets.

10.2 Path to International Markets

Year 1: Focus exclusively on US market. Build 3–5 pilot race case studies. Achieve BPI certification. Year 2: Pursue EN 13432 certification. Approach London Marathon (TCS-sponsored, same parent as NYC), Berlin, and Toronto (already eliminated mylar, uses ASTM D6400). Year 3: Appoint regional distributors for EU and Australia. Tokyo requires a Japanese distribution partner due to language and regulatory complexity.
