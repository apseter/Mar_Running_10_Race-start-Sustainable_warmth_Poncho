P7F: SEASONAL MARKETING

CAMPAIGNS

Sub-Project P7F | Marketing, PR & Community | Weeks 16–Ongoing

Complete Seasonal Strategy: Fall 2026 Campaign, Spring 2027 Campaign, Holiday Gift Campaign, Race-Week Flash Sales, Earth Day Activation, 12-Month Race Calendar Content Map, and Year 1 Marketing ROI Report Framework

March 2026 | Confidential

Executive Summary

Marketing for a seasonal product requires seasonal campaigns. The poncho’s demand is driven by the race calendar: 55–65% in fall (Sep–Nov), 25–35% in spring (Mar–May), and the remainder from holiday gifting and off-season preparation. Every marketing dollar must be timed to when runners are thinking about their next race — not scattered evenly across 12 months. This document structures ALL marketing activity into: 5 defined campaigns (Fall, Spring, Holiday, Earth Day, and Race-Week Flash Sales) mapped onto a 12-month race calendar content plan, with a Year 1 Marketing ROI Report framework that measures performance across all 7 marketing channels.

The annual marketing rhythm: Fall campaign (Sep–Nov) is the LAUNCH and BIGGEST push: first pilot races, first case studies, highest ad spend, maximum content velocity. Holiday campaign (Nov–Dec) extends the selling season with gift positioning. Spring campaign (Feb–May) leverages fall results to accelerate spring race partnerships. Earth Day (April 22) is the annual sustainability moment for maximum PR impact. Race-week flash sales (throughout) drive urgency around every major race weekend. The 12-month calendar ensures there is ALWAYS a campaign running, a race to prepare for, or a moment to leverage.

1. Fall 2026 Campaign: “The Cold Is Coming”

Fall is the make-or-break season. The brand launches into the running world during the 13-week window that contains: the 4 largest US cold-weather marathons (Chicago, MCM, NYC, Philadelphia), the highest DTC purchase intent (runners actively preparing for fall races), and the strongest B2B conversion environment (pilot races executing, case studies being produced, expo booths generating leads). Every marketing system built in P5–P7 converges on this window.

Element Specification
Campaign name “Fall Race Season 2026”
Duration September 1 – November 30 (13 weeks)
Key races Chicago Marathon (Oct 11), Marine Corps (Oct 27), Detroit (Oct 19), Twin Cities (Oct 4), NYC Marathon (Nov 1), Philadelphia (Nov 22), Richmond (Nov 15), Sacramento CIM (Dec 7)
Campaign theme “The cold is coming. Be ready.” — builds anticipation from September, peaks during October–November race weekends, and winds down post-Thanksgiving.
Social content plan Weeks 1–4 (Sep): daily fall-themed content. Waste stats infographics for Chicago + Twin Cities + Detroit. Countdown timers for early-October races. Ambassador race-day previews. Start Line Stories featuring fall memories. Weeks 5–8 (Oct): PEAK CONTENT. Race-specific posts for each weekend’s major marathon. Live race-day content from pilot races (P5B). Post-race composting data. Ambassador race-day Reels. Weeks 9–13 (Nov): NYC Marathon content surge (largest race of the year). Post-NYC composting report. Transition to Thanksgiving messaging (“Thankful for runners who choose sustainability”). Tease: holiday gift campaign.
Email plan Sep 1: “Fall race season is HERE.” Welcome to the season, link to race-specific landing pages. Race-week emails (P7B Section 8) for EACH major race: 6-week, 4-week, 2-week, race-week, race-day, post-race sequence. Oct–Nov: weekly newsletter with: race recaps, composting data, ambassador spotlights, and product promotions. Black Friday (Nov 27): early access email to subscribers with exclusive discount.
Paid ads Per P7D Section 5 budget concentration: $20–40/day during Sep–Nov peak. Race-weekend surge campaigns for Chicago, MCM, NYC, Philadelphia. Creative: race-specific urgency ads + UGC video + waste stats carousel. Retargeting at maximum frequency during race weeks.
B2B integration Fall pilot races execute (P5B). Post-race debriefs and case studies produced. B2B sample kits distributed at expos (P5D). Sponsor outreach peaks: sponsors planning next year’s budgets in Q4 respond to fall pitch decks (P5E).
KPIs DTC units sold (target: 500–1,500 across Amazon + Shopify). B2B units shipped (target: 3,000–10,000 across pilot + early-adopter races). Email list growth (target: +500–1,000 subscribers). Social followers (target: +1,000–2,000 across Instagram + TikTok). Revenue (target: $8,000–$25,000 DTC + $10,000–$40,000 B2B).

2. Spring 2027 Campaign: “New Season. Same Warmth.”

Spring is the SECOND WAVE that proves the brand is not a one-season wonder. Spring marketing benefits from: real fall data (composting reports, runner surveys, case studies), refined creative (fall testing identified winning ads and content), a growing email list (fall sign-ups become spring customers), and an expanding B2B pipeline (fall pilot race directors reorder + referrals generate new spring partners).

Creative refresh: new UGC from fall ambassadors, updated waste stats with real data, and spring-themed visuals (cherry blossoms for DC, dogwoods for Boston).

3. Holiday Gift Campaign: “The Runner’s Gift Guide”

The holiday campaign extends the poncho beyond race season into year-round gifting. Runners receive gifts from non-runners who search: “gifts for marathon runners,” “stocking stuffers for runners,” and “sustainable gifts.” The 3-pack at $24.99 fits perfectly in the $20–$30 gift bracket: thoughtful, unique, sustainable, and useful. The holiday campaign transforms a seasonal product into a year-round brand.

Element Specification
Campaign name “The Runner’s Gift Guide”
Duration November 15 – December 25 (6 weeks)
Theme “The perfect gift for the runner who has everything.” Position the 3-pack as a thoughtful, unique, eco-friendly stocking stuffer or gift for running friends. Holiday messaging: “Give warmth. Leave no trace. The gift that keeps runners warm AND composts after the race.”
Product focus 3-PACK ($24.99) as the PRIMARY gift SKU. Price point is ideal for: stocking stuffers ($25 budget), running club Secret Santa, and “gift for the runner in your life” search. Consider: limited-edition holiday packaging (kraft gift box with ribbon, $0.50–$1.00 premium — only if volume justifies the packaging run).
Email sequence Email 1 (Nov 15): “Gift guide for runners: 5 ideas they’ll actually love.” Poncho = #1 pick. Email 2 (Dec 1): “25 days to Christmas. Order by [date] for delivery.” Urgency + free shipping. Email 3 (Dec 10): “Last call for holiday delivery.” Final shipping cutoff. Email 4 (Dec 20): “Digital gift card available instantly.” For last-minute shoppers: Shopify gift card sent via email ($10, $25, $50 denominations).
Paid ads Holiday-specific creatives: “Gift for the runner who has everything” static image (poncho in a gift box with a bow). Target: audiences interested in running + gift-giving (Meta’s “Engaged Shoppers” behavior targeting). Google Shopping: activate for “running gift,” “marathon gift,” “sustainable gift for runners” keywords. Budget: $10–20/day for 4 weeks (Dec 1–25).
Social content Gift guide carousel (“5 Gifts for the Runner in Your Life” — poncho featured alongside other running brands’ products for broader appeal). Unboxing videos: ambassador unboxing the holiday packaging. “Why this is the most thoughtful running gift” explainer Reel. Customer testimonials: “I gave this to my running partner and she loved it.”
Revenue target 200–500 3-packs sold in the 6-week holiday window. At $24.99: $5,000–$12,500 holiday revenue. If the holiday campaign generates 15–20% of annual DTC revenue: the poncho has successfully extended beyond a pure race-season product into a year-round gifting item.

4. Race-Week Flash Sales

Flash sales create micro-urgency around every major race weekend. A runner browsing the expo booth on Saturday who didn’t buy can purchase online Saturday night at the same price. A subscriber who has been considering the product sees “24-hour Race Weekend Special” and finally commits. Flash sales convert the race-weekend energy into DTC revenue.

Element Specification
Trigger Major race expo opens (Thursday or Friday before a Sunday race)
Duration 24–48 hours (aligned with expo days)
Offer “Race Weekend Flash Sale: 3-Pack for $19.99” (20% off, same as expo pricing per P5D Section 6). Or: “Free shipping on ALL orders this weekend.” The offer MATCHES the expo pricing so runners who saw the product at the expo but didn’t buy can purchase online at the same price.
Channels Email blast to: all subscribers in the race city (geographic segment) + all subscribers who visited the race-specific landing page (behavioral segment). Instagram Story + feed post with countdown timer. TikTok: “Race weekend flash sale — link in bio.” Meta ad: race-weekend urgency creative (P7D Section 2 Creative 6) at surge budget.
Landing page Shopify discount code (auto-applied via URL: brand.com/flash?discount=RACEWEEKEND20). Landing page shows: the discounted 3-pack, a countdown timer to sale end, and “Ships Monday — arrives before your next race.”
Post-sale Email to purchasers: “Thanks for your race-weekend order! Good luck this weekend. Tag us in your start-line photo: #RaceWarmLeaveNoTrace.” Track: flash sale revenue, units sold, discount code redemptions, new vs. returning customers. Feed data into: P7D monthly ad performance review.
Expected results Per flash sale event: 20–50 3-packs sold ($400–$1,000 revenue). At 5–8 flash sales per fall season: $2,000–$8,000 in flash sale revenue. Flash sales also drive: email list engagement (subscribers learn to watch for sales), social urgency (followers see time-limited offers), and expo-to-online conversion (runners who browsed at the booth purchase online).

5. Earth Day Campaign (April 22)

Earth Day is the ONE DAY per year when the entire media landscape focuses on sustainability. Every news outlet, every brand, and every consumer is thinking about environmental impact on April 22. For a sustainability brand: Earth Day is the equivalent of Black Friday — the day when the brand’s message resonates most broadly and reaches audiences who might not otherwise engage with sustainability content.

Element Specification
Date April 22 (annual)
Theme “Every poncho composted is a garbage bag that didn’t reach the landfill.” Earth Day messaging centers on: the cumulative impact (total waste diverted to date), the individual action (each poncho purchase is a choice), and the systemic change (replacing single-use plastic at athletic events).
Content IMPACT REPORT: publish the annual sustainability report (cumulative composting data from all races to date) on brand.com/impact. Share key stats as: Instagram carousel (“Our Year 1 impact: [X] ponchos composted, [Y] lbs diverted, [Z] races served”), TikTok video (time-lapse of composting footage + impact data overlay), and email to entire list (“Your impact on Earth Day: here’s what WE’VE accomplished together”). LIMITED EDITION: consider a special Earth Day colorway (e.g., “Ocean Blue” or “Forest Green” per P2F Section 5 color variations) or a co-branded poncho with an environmental organization (1% for the Planet, Leave No Trace per P5C Section 5).
Promotion “Earth Day Special: plant a tree with every 3-pack purchase.” Partner with: One Tree Planted ($1/tree, onetreeplanted.org) to plant a tree for every 3-pack sold during Earth Week (Apr 20–26). Cost: $1 per 3-pack × 50–100 estimated sales = $50–$100. Marketing value: “Your poncho composts AND plants a tree” is a powerful Earth Day message. Display: “Tree planted!” badge on the product page during Earth Week.
PR Issue an Earth Day press release: “[BRAND] Publishes Year 1 Sustainability Report: [X] lbs of Race-Day Waste Composted.” Pitch to: sustainability media (P7C Section 3), local news (Earth Day angle), and running media (“How the running industry is getting greener”). Earth Day is the HIGHEST-VOLUME sustainability news day of the year — media outlets actively seek Earth Day stories.
Social activation Instagram: post the impact report infographic + a “Thank you to every runner who chose sustainability” message. TikTok: “What we’ve composted so far” video with rising counter animation. Strava Club: Earth Day challenge (“Run 4.22 miles on April 22 — post your Strava activity with #RaceWarmLeaveNoTrace”). Reddit: post the impact report in r/sustainability and r/ZeroWaste (genuine contribution, not promotion — the data IS the value).

9. Conclusion: The Calendar Is the Strategy

A seasonal product doesn’t need a marketing “strategy” in the abstract sense. It needs a CALENDAR. When is the next race? What are runners thinking about right now? What content serves them today? Every marketing decision flows from the race calendar: September means Chicago Marathon content, October means NYC Marathon surge, December means holiday gifts, April means Boston Marathon + Earth Day. The founder who opens the 12-month map on January 1 and follows it week by week will never miss a race, never scramble for content, and never wonder “what should we post today?”

Year 1 marketing ROI will likely be 5–10× blended across all channels. Email will be the highest ROI at 36–42×. Content/SEO will compound from 0 to 10–50× over 12 months. Ambassadors will deliver 3–8× in direct + indirect value. Paid ads will be the lowest ROI at 1.2–2.5× but will serve the critical function of accelerating first-90-day momentum. The Year 2 plan reallocates: more to content, SEO, and email (compounding channels), same to ambassadors and community (relationship channels), and more EFFICIENT paid spend (better creative, larger retargeting pools, higher organic baseline reduces paid dependency). The flywheel doesn’t need more fuel each year — it needs the SAME fuel spinning a LARGER wheel.
