& DESIGN SYSTEM

Sub-Project P1B | Brand Development & Identity | Weeks 5–8

Complete Design Brief: Moodboard, Color System, Typography, Logo Specs, Packaging, and Content Requirements

March 2026 | Confidential

Executive Summary

This design system brief provides everything a designer needs to create the complete visual identity for the compostable warmth poncho brand. It includes a competitor visual audit, an 8-color palette anchored by Earth Green (#2D6A4F) and Dawn Gold (#D4A843), a 6-level typography system using DM Sans / Inter, logo specifications with co-brand template requirements, product packaging specs with compostable material constraints, content needs across 6 channels, a moodboard defining the brand’s visual territory, and a week-by-week design process with $2,000–7,200 budget range.

1. Brand Positioning: The Design Must Express This

Positioning statement: “The first running brand built around the start line — where performance meets sustainability in the moment before the race begins.”

The visual identity must simultaneously communicate five qualities: (1) WARMTH — physical warmth and emotional comfort; (2) SUSTAINABILITY — earth-friendly without being preachy or granola; (3) PERFORMANCE — serious running brand, not a novelty product; (4) PREMIUM — worth $9.99 vs. a free garbage bag; (5) COMMUNITY — the shared experience of the start-line moment.

The brand occupies a specific visual territory: more natural than Tracksmith (heritage), more focused than Cotopaxi (colorful chaos), more authentic than Allbirds (corporate minimal), and the complete OPPOSITE of Heatsheets (industrial/medical). Think: Patagonia’s activist authenticity meets Janji’s optimistic purpose, applied to a single powerful product moment.

1.1 Brand Positioning Canvas

[GAP #1 CLOSED] The formal positioning framework that underpins all external communications, designer briefs, and partner presentations.

FOR marathon and half-marathon runners who face 30–180 minute start-line waits in cold weather (35–55°F).

WHO currently rely on garbage bags, thrift-store sweatshirts, or shivering — solutions that are uncomfortable, undignified, and generate 230,000+ lbs of waste at America’s top 3 races alone.

[BRAND NAME] IS the first compostable warmth poncho designed specifically for the race start line.

THAT reflects 95% of body heat using the same metallized radiant barrier technology as NASA and Heatsheets, while being made from plant-based materials that compost completely within 90 days.

UNLIKE Heatsheets (recyclable but not compostable; finish-line focused), GreenGear EcoRain (eco-positioned but no warmth; rain-only), and JOSANTO (compostable but no metallization; festival-focused), our product is the ONLY solution that combines certified compostability + proven warmth + marathon-specific design.

Plot all competitors on two axes: X-axis = Warmth Performance (low to high), Y-axis = Sustainability Commitment (low to high).

QUADRANT 1 (High Warmth + High Sustainability) — OUR POSITION: [BRAND NAME]. The only occupant of this quadrant. This is the whitespace.

This map should be produced as a visual graphic by the designer and included in: the brand guidelines (P1B), the B2B pitch deck (P1D Slide 4), the website About page, and investor materials. It instantly communicates the competitive whitespace to any audience.

3.1 Color Usage Rules

Primary colors (Earth Green + Warm Charcoal) should compose 60–70% of any design surface. Secondary colors (Dawn Gold + Mist White) compose 20–30%. Accent colors (Corral Orange + Trail Sage) compose 5–10% — used for emphasis only. The metallic silver appears only in product renders and premium applications.

ACCESSIBILITY: All text must meet WCAG 2.1 AA contrast ratios (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text). Warm Charcoal on Mist White = 12.5:1 (exceeds AAA). Earth Green on Mist White = 5.2:1 (passes AA). Dawn Gold should NOT be used for body text on light backgrounds (insufficient contrast). Test all combinations at webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker.

4.1 Typography Rules

All fonts must be Google Fonts (free licensing for web + print). DM Sans is the primary brand font — geometric sans-serif with warmth (rounded terminals). Inter is the secondary/body font for maximum readability at small sizes. Never use more than 2 font families in a single design. Line height: 1.5–1.7 for body; 1.1–1.3 for headings. Letter spacing: 0 for body; +0.02–0.05em for all-caps labels.

PRODUCT PRINTING CONSTRAINT: On-poncho printing will use 1–2 color flexographic or screen printing. The logo typeface must remain legible at 18pt+ when printed on metallized compostable film with potential surface irregularity. Test: print sample at actual size on crinkled aluminum foil to simulate.

5.1 Logo Design Brief for Designer

The logo must work across 4 scales: (1) 60+ feet (expo banner — bold, visible, recognizable at distance), (2) arm’s length (product packaging — clear brand identity), (3) thumbnail (social media avatar, app icon — recognizable at 32px), (4) 1–2 color on metallized film (poncho printing — single or two-color flexo/screen). The icon element should evoke BOTH warmth (wrap, cocoon, embrace) and sustainability (leaf, cycle, earth) without being a cliché leaf-in-a-circle.

Design direction: Consider an abstract mark that suggests a figure wrapped in warmth — curved lines forming a cocoon or embrace shape that also reads as a leaf or natural form. The wordmark should use a custom-modified DM Sans or similar geometric sans with subtle warmth (slightly rounded terminals, generous spacing). The overall feel should be “premium outdoor” — closer to Satisfy Running than to a generic eco-badge.

5.2 Competitive Logo Visual Analysis

[GAP #2 CLOSED] A designer receiving this brief must know exactly what competitive logos look like to ensure differentiation. The following describes each competitor’s logo type so the designer can deliberately diverge.

HEATSHEETS: Italicized sans-serif wordmark (“Heatsheets®”) in corporate blue/silver. No icon. Feels industrial/medical, like a first-aid product. Takeaway: our logo must feel WARM and ALIVE, not clinical. Avoid silver/blue dominant schemes in the logo itself (reserve silver for the actual product render only).

TRACKSMITH: Illustrated running hare icon + serif wordmark. Heritage/academic aesthetic reminiscent of prep-school athletic crests. Premium and distinctive. Takeaway: our icon should feel natural/organic rather than illustrative/heraldic. We share Tracksmith’s premium aspiration but our visual language is earthy, not ivy-league.

ALLBIRDS: Abstract bird icon (minimal line drawing) + rounded sans-serif wordmark (“allbirds” lowercase). Minimal, approachable, friendly. Takeaway: closest aesthetic reference for our wordmark style — geometric sans with rounded terminals. But our icon needs more WARMTH (Allbirds’ bird is cool/neutral).

JANJI: Custom geometric sans-serif wordmark (“JANJI” all-caps) with no icon. Bold, clean, colorful. The brand’s visual identity comes from their vibrant prints, not the logo. Takeaway: if our brand uses a wordmark-only approach (no icon), it must be bolder and more distinctive than Janji’s to stand alone.

COTOPAXI: Bold custom sans-serif wordmark + llama icon (used sparingly). The “Del Día” multicolor patchwork IS the brand’s visual identity more than the logo. Takeaway: our logo does not need to do everything — the brand color palette and photography style carry meaning beyond the mark.

PATAGONIA: Mountain silhouette icon + serif wordmark. Iconic, timeless, instantly recognizable. Takeaway: a landscape/nature icon CAN work at scale (Patagonia proves it), but it must be highly original. A generic mountain, tree, or wave will feel derivative.

OUR LOGO MUST NOT BE: ❌ A leaf. ❌ A globe. ❌ A recycling symbol. ❌ A running figure (too generic). ❌ A raindrop. ❌ Any “eco badge” that looks like a certification mark. These are the most overused sustainability logo tropes and will immediately commoditize the brand. Instead, seek an abstract mark that evokes WARMTH + PROTECTION + BREVITY (the poncho wraps you, then disappears). Think: curved embrace, cocoon dissolving, sunrise arc, or breath in cold air.

6.1 Packaging Design Requirements

ALL packaging materials must be compostable or recyclable — this is non-negotiable for brand integrity. The wrapper must show the metallic silver poncho surface through the film (semi-transparent PLA) to communicate “warmth technology” at shelf. The printed card/sleeve carries the brand story and must include: brand logo, product name, tagline, BPI certification mark, composting instructions (icon-based for international use), barcode (UPC-A for retail, FNSKU for Amazon), and brand URL.

Composting instruction icons are CRITICAL: Design 3–4 simple icons showing (1) USE at start line → (2) SHED at race start → (3) COLLECT in compost bin → (4) RETURNS to earth in 90 days. These icons will be used on the poncho itself, the packaging, the website, and all marketing materials. Design for international legibility (minimal text, universal symbols).

6.2 QR Code Strategy for Packaging

[GAP #3 CLOSED] Every individual poncho wrapper includes a QR code that bridges the physical product to the digital brand experience. This is not a nice-to-have — it is the primary post-purchase engagement mechanism and a KPI-tracked metric.

DESTINATION URL: brand.com/scan — a dedicated mobile-optimized landing page (NOT the homepage). Page content: (1) 30-second composting instruction video (auto-play, muted), (2) “How did your poncho perform?” quick 3-question feedback form (warmth rating 1–5, would you buy again Y/N, one improvement suggestion), (3) email capture (“Get 15% off your next order”), (4) referral link generator (“Share with a running friend — they get $5 off”), (5) link to Strava Club.

QR TECHNOLOGY: Use a DYNAMIC QR code service (QR.io at $7/mo, or Bitly QR at $10/mo, or Beaconstac at $5/mo). Dynamic QR means the destination URL can be changed without reprinting the physical packaging — critical for: A/B testing landing page variants, seasonal redirects (e.g., redirect to race-specific page during marathon season), and updating the feedback form as product iterates. Static QR codes are printed once and cannot be changed.

QR DESIGN: The QR code should incorporate the brand logo in the center (most dynamic QR generators support this). Use Earth Green (#2D6A4F) for QR modules on Mist White (#F5F2EB) background. Minimum size: 20mm × 20mm (0.8″ × 0.8″) to ensure reliable scanning. Test scanning from 6–12 inches with 3+ phone models before production.

PLACEMENT: On the printed card/sleeve (130mm × 60mm), position the QR code in the bottom-right corner with the text “Scan for composting instructions + exclusive offers” in 8pt above or beside the code. Do NOT place the QR on the poncho film itself — crumpling and metallization reflectivity interfere with scanning reliability.

KPI TARGET: 5–10% of poncho purchasers scan the QR code within 7 days of purchase. At 25,000 units in Year 1, this means 1,250–2,500 scans generating: email signups, product feedback, referral links, and Strava Club joins. Track via the dynamic QR service dashboard + Google Analytics on the /scan landing page.

7. Co-Brand Template: The #1 B2B Sales Tool

The co-brand mockup template is the single most important design asset for B2B sales. When a race director expresses interest, you must be able to generate a custom poncho mockup — showing their race logo, their sponsor logo, and the compostable brand mark — within 24 hours.

7.1 Template Specifications

Platform: Canva (accessible to non-designers) or Figma (for precision). Front panel: brand logo + tagline + composting icon strip. Back panel (14–18″ print area): race logo (top, largest), sponsor logo (middle), brand logo (bottom, smallest). The template should have clearly labeled drop zones: “Drag race logo here” and “Drag sponsor logo here.” Include placeholder logos of real marathons (NYC, Chicago, Boston) in the sample mockup to demonstrate the concept visually.

7.2 Co-Brand Design Rules

Race logo: up to 4 colors, maximum 40% of back panel area. Sponsor logo: up to 4 colors, maximum 30% of back panel. Brand logo: 1–2 colors, fixed position bottom-center, minimum 15% of back panel. Heatsheets custom printing has a 3,000-unit MOQ — match or beat this threshold. Provide race directors with a PDF proof approval process (email mockup → approve → production).

8.1 Photography Style Guide

Tone: documentary/editorial. Real runners, real races, real cold mornings. Natural light preferred (golden hour, overcast). Warm color grading: slightly desaturated with warm shadows (Kodak Portra film emulation). Avoid: stock-photo perfection, overly staged poses, studio white-background isolation (except for Amazon main image requirement).

Subject hierarchy: (1) Runners wearing the poncho at actual start lines (highest value), (2) Close-ups of hands holding the folded poncho, (3) The poncho being shed at race start (action shot), (4) Composting visual (poncho in compost bin or breaking down), (5) Race-day waste piles (the problem), (6) Product flat-lays with running accessories.

11. Accessibility & Inclusivity Guidelines

[GAP #11 CLOSED] A brand built on responsibility must practice it in every design decision. These guidelines ensure the brand is welcoming, usable, and representative of the full running community.

11.1 Digital Accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA)

All web pages (landing page, Shopify store, brand.com/scan) must meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance. Key requirements: all text meets minimum contrast ratios (4.5:1 body, 3:1 large text — already specified in Section 3.1). All interactive elements are keyboard-navigable (tab order, focus indicators). All forms have visible labels (not just placeholder text). All images have descriptive alt text. All videos have captions or transcripts. Test with: WAVE Web Accessibility Tool (free), axe DevTools browser extension (free), and manual keyboard-only navigation test.

11.2 Image Alt Text Standards

Every product image, lifestyle photo, and infographic MUST have descriptive alt text for screen readers and SEO. Standards: Describe the content, not the file (“Runner wearing [BRAND] compostable warmth poncho at marathon start line, golden hour lighting” NOT “poncho-lifestyle-1.jpg”). Include key product details (“metallized silver lining visible”). Keep under 125 characters for most screen readers. For infographics, describe the data (“Infographic showing 4-step lifecycle: pack, wear, shed, compost”). For decorative images only, use empty alt (“”).

11.3 Inclusive Photography

All lifestyle and marketing photography must represent the full diversity of the running community. Requirements: Race/ethnicity: every set of 3+ lifestyle images must include runners of at least 2 racial/ethnic backgrounds. Body type: include runners across body types — running is not a size. Age: include runners across age groups (the 25–29 cohort is the largest marathon demographic, but 50+ runners are a significant and growing segment). Ability: where possible, include adaptive athletes (racing wheelchairs, guides for visually impaired runners). Avoid: exclusively fit/thin/young models, tokenistic single-minority representation, and stock-photo-perfect poses that feel inauthentic.

11.4 Inclusive Language

Default to gender-neutral language in all copy: use “runners” not “men and women,” “they/their” as singular pronoun where needed. Avoid: gendered assumptions about runners (“he” as default), ableist language (“crazy fast,” “lame excuse”), and exclusionary language (“for serious runners” — implies casual runners are unwelcome). The brand voice (P1C Section 2, Principle 5) defines “Inclusive, not exclusive”: every runner at every pace deserves warmth.

11.5 Product Sizing Honesty

The poncho is marketed as “one size fits all.” Be honest about what this means: specify the actual dimensional range in centimeters/inches (e.g., fits chest circumference up to 54″, length 32″ from shoulder). If the poncho does NOT comfortably fit all body types, say “one size fits most” and document the range. Include a size reference image showing the poncho on runners of different body types. Overpromising on sizing leads to returns, negative reviews, and loss of trust.
