MARKETING

Sub-Project P7B | Marketing, PR & Community | Weeks 14–Ongoing

Complete Content Strategy: Weekly Content Calendar, 4-Pillar Content Mix, Start-Line Waste Stats Series, 6 Video Content Templates, TikTok/Reels Strategy, 10+ Blog Plan, Sustainability Checklist Lead Magnet, and 6-Stage Race-Week Email Campaign

March 2026 | Confidential

Executive Summary

Content marketing is the engine that turns strangers into followers, followers into customers, and customers into advocates. Unlike paid advertising (which stops when the budget stops), content compounds: a blog post published in Week 14 drives traffic in Month 12. A TikTok Reel about the tear-away demo can go viral and generate 100,000 impressions from $0 in ad spend. An infographic about start-line waste gets shared by running communities, earning backlinks that boost SEO for the entire site.

This document builds the complete content system: a weekly 3-day posting calendar (Monday/Wednesday/Friday + race-week bonus), a 4-pillar content mix balancing sustainability science (30%), runner culture (30%), product in action (25%), and impact/transparency (15%), a 5-race Start-Line Waste Stats infographic series, 6 video content templates including the viral tear-away demo, a 6-format TikTok/Reels strategy, a 10+ blog publication plan across 6 months, a Race Day Sustainability Checklist PDF as an email-capture lead magnet, and a 6-stage race-week email campaign timed from 6 weeks to race day.

1. Content Calendar: 3 Posts Per Week

Consistency matters more than volume. 3 posts per week (Monday, Wednesday, Friday) is sustainable for a solo founder and sufficient to build audience + algorithmic momentum on Instagram and TikTok. Each day has a THEME that aligns with the 4-pillar content mix (Section 2): Monday = Educate, Wednesday = Inspire, Friday = Engage. Race weeks add a bonus SELL post.

Day Theme Content Example Platform
Monday EDUCATE Sustainability fact, composting tip, or waste statistic. Example: “Did you know? 35,000+ garbage bags are discarded at the start line of a single marathon. That’s [X] lbs of LDPE going straight to landfill.” Format: single-image infographic or carousel with data from P0A. Instagram feed post (carousel or single image). Cross-post to: LinkedIn (text + image), Facebook page.
Wednesday INSPIRE Runner story, ambassador spotlight, race-day photo, or founder update. Example: “Meet Sarah — she ran the Chicago Marathon in a compostable poncho and composted it right at the start line. Here’s her story.” Format: photo + caption or short video. Instagram feed post or Reel. Cross-post to: TikTok (if video), Facebook, Strava Club.
Friday ENGAGE Interactive content: poll (“What’s your worst start-line cold story?”), question (“What temperature makes you want a poncho?”), or meme/humor (“Marathon runners in the corral at 5 AM be like...” with a relatable image). Format: Story poll/question sticker, carousel, or Reel. Instagram Story (poll/question). Cross-post: TikTok (if video), Facebook Story.
Race week (bonus) SELL Product-focused: “[Race Name] is this weekend! Order now for race-day warmth. Ships free on 3-packs.” Countdown, urgency, direct CTA. Format: Story with “Shop Now” link, feed post with product shot. Instagram Story + feed post. Cross-post: email (race-week campaign, Section 7), Facebook, TikTok.

1.1 Content Batch Production

BATCH CREATION: instead of creating content daily (stressful, inconsistent), batch-produce 1–2 weeks of content in a single 2–3 hour session. WORKFLOW: (1) Sunday evening: plan the week’s 3 posts using the calendar template. (2) Monday morning: create all 3 posts (write captions, design graphics in Canva, edit video clips). (3) Schedule: use Shopify’s built-in social scheduling, Later ($18/month, recommended for visual planning), or Buffer ($6/month per channel). (4) Race weeks: add the bonus SELL post and schedule race-week emails (Section 7) on the same day. TOTAL TIME: 2–3 hours/week for all social content creation and scheduling. This is manageable for a solo founder alongside all other operational responsibilities.

2. Content Pillars: 4-Category Mix

Content pillars prevent the brand from becoming a one-note product account. A running brand that only posts about its product is boring. A running brand that posts about sustainability science, runner culture, product experiences, AND composting impact is interesting. The 4-pillar mix ensures: the brand speaks to multiple audience motivations (eco-conscious, community-seeking, gear-curious, impact-driven) while maintaining a cohesive identity.

Pillar % of Content Content Types Strategic Purpose
SUSTAINABILITY SCIENCE 30% Waste statistics by race (P0A data), composting process explained, LDPE vs. compostable comparison, BPI certification meaning, circular economy concepts, ocean plastic impact. Tone: informative, data-driven, no preaching. Let the numbers speak. Establishes topical AUTHORITY. Google and social algorithms favor accounts that consistently produce expert-level content on a specific topic. Every sustainability post strengthens the brand’s position as the running industry’s sustainability voice.
RUNNER CULTURE 30% Start-line traditions, corral cold stories, race-day rituals, training updates, running humor, race recaps, runner community moments. Tone: relatable, funny, warm. This is the content that makes runners feel “this brand GETS me.” Builds COMMUNITY. Runners follow brands that speak their language. A brand that only posts about its product is boring. A brand that posts about the runner EXPERIENCE — and happens to make a product — earns followers who become customers.
PRODUCT IN ACTION 25% Product demos (tear-away, warmth test, unboxing), race-day footage of runners in ponchos, co-brand reveals, packaging process, factory/production behind-the-scenes. Tone: honest, visual, educational. Drives CONVERSION. This is the content that turns awareness into purchases. But it’s only 25% of the mix — if every post is a product pitch, followers tune out. The 30/30/25 balance ensures the product content lands on an audience that already trusts and relates to the brand.
IMPACT + TRANSPARENCY 15% Composting results from pilot races, waste diverted updates (impact counter data), facility tours, annual sustainability report highlights, “where does your poncho go?” stories, behind-the-scenes of composting partnerships. Tone: transparent, proud but humble, data-backed. Builds TRUST. Transparency is the antidote to greenwashing skepticism. Every post showing REAL composting data, REAL facility photos, and REAL weight-diverted numbers tells the audience: “This brand does what it says.”

2.1 Pillar-to-Day Mapping

MONDAY (Educate) draws from: Sustainability Science (primary) and Impact + Transparency. WEDNESDAY (Inspire) draws from: Runner Culture (primary) and Product in Action. FRIDAY (Engage) draws from: Runner Culture (primary) and any pillar. RACE WEEK (Sell) draws from: Product in Action. This mapping is a GUIDELINE, not a rigid rule. If a great runner-culture post idea comes on Monday, post it Monday. Authenticity trumps scheduling. The pillar ratios should average out over a month, not be enforced per week.

Each infographic quantifies the start-line waste problem at a specific major marathon, making the abstract (“race-day waste is bad”) concrete (“35,000 garbage bags at the NYC Marathon”). The series builds: brand authority on the waste issue, race-specific relevance (NYC runners share the NYC infographic), and a library of shareable assets that earn backlinks from sustainability blogs.

3.1 Infographic Design Template

Create a REUSABLE Canva template for the series: consistent layout across all 5 infographics. Format: 1080×1080px (Instagram square) or 1080×1350px (portrait, more screen real estate on mobile). Elements: race-city silhouette or landmark illustration (top), bold stat number (center, large DM Sans font), explanatory text (bottom), brand logo + #RaceWarmLeaveNoTrace (footer). Color: Earth Green background with Mist White text, or Mist White background with Warm Charcoal text. The TEMPLATE approach ensures: (A) each new infographic takes <30 minutes to produce (swap city illustration + stats), (B) the series has a cohesive visual identity that’s recognizable in the feed, and (C) the template is reusable for: new races, updated data, and Year 2 comparisons (“Before [BRAND]: X garbage bags. After: Y ponchos composted”).

4. Video Content: 6 Templates

Video is the highest-engagement format on every platform. Instagram Reels get 2–3× the reach of static posts. TikTok videos reach audiences far beyond the brand’s followers. YouTube Shorts surface in Google search results. The 6 video templates below cover the full product story: demo, comparison, behind-the-scenes, testimonial, composting proof, and first-person experience.

Video Shot List Notes
Tear-away demo (15–30 sec) Close-up of hands wearing the poncho. Pull the tab. The perforation tears cleanly. Poncho drops away in <3 seconds. Slow-motion replay of the tear. Satisfying ASMR-style audio of the ripping sound. This is the #1 viral content opportunity. The tear sound, the clean line, the speed — it’s inherently satisfying and shareable. TikTok/Reels viewers love “oddly satisfying” content. Film from multiple angles: front, side, overhead, POV (wearing GoPro or phone on chest).
Corral simulation (30–60 sec) Set up in a parking lot or park at dawn (5–6 AM, cold, low light). Gather 3–5 runners. Half wear garbage bags, half wear the poncho. Compare: movement, warmth (interview: “how do you feel?”), appearance, and ease of removal at “the gun” (someone blows a horn). Side-by-side split-screen. This video is the COMPARATIVE proof: poncho vs. garbage bag in a realistic setting. The visual contrast (silver metallized poncho vs. black garbage bag) is instantly compelling. Use FLIR camera if available for a thermal comparison overlay.
Composting time-lapse (60–90 sec) Time-lapse of the poncho decomposing in a commercial composting facility over 12–24 weeks. Compressed to 60 seconds: intact poncho → beginning to break down → fragments → soil. Overlay: week counter (“Week 1... Week 4... Week 12... Week 24”). End: hands holding finished compost with a seedling growing. This requires: coordination with a composting facility to document the actual poncho over months. START THIS EARLY (Week 14–16): place 2–3 ponchos in a facility and photograph weekly. The time-lapse won’t be ready until Month 4–6 but will be the most shared piece of content the brand produces. It is VISUAL PROOF of the sustainability promise.
Race-day POV (30–60 sec) GoPro or chest-mounted phone POV: put on the poncho in the corral, wait with thousands of other runners, hear the gun, tear off the poncho, start running. First-person perspective of the entire product experience. POV content is immersive. The viewer FEELS the experience: the cold corral, the nervousness, the poncho warmth, the gun, the tear, the freedom. This converts because it answers the question: “What is it actually like to use this product?”
Behind-the-scenes: factory (60–90 sec) Tour of the factory (Parkside, ShinHigh, or ProAmpac): raw material rolls, lamination machine, laser perforation, printing, folding, packaging. Narration: “Here’s how your poncho is made — from raw cellulose to race-ready in 4 weeks.” Behind-the-scenes builds trust and differentiates from generic imports. Viewers see: the product is MANUFACTURED with intention, not mass-produced in a faceless factory. Request: factory permission to film during a production visit (P3A).
Runner testimonial compilation (60 sec) 3–5 runner clips (10–15 sec each) answering: “How was the poncho?” Shot at: the finish line, post-race food area, or expo. Quick-cut style. Runners: diverse ages, genders, race types. End with: the brand logo and “Race Warm. Leave No Trace.” Compilation testimonials are social proof in video form. The diversity of runners shows: the product works for EVERYONE, not just elite athletes or eco-purists. Collect clips at every pilot race (P5B) and expo (P5D).

4.1 Video Production Setup

EQUIPMENT (budget): smartphone (iPhone 12+ or equivalent Android for 4K video), gimbal stabilizer ($50–$100: DJI OM 6 or Zhiyun Smooth 5), clip-on lavalier microphone ($15–$30: Boya BY-M1 for testimonial audio), portable LED light ($20–40: for indoor/low-light shots). TOTAL: $85–$170 in equipment (all reusable). Editing: CapCut (free, mobile, excellent for Reels/TikTok), DaVinci Resolve (free desktop, professional-grade), or InShot ($4/month, simple mobile editor). NO professional videographer needed for Season 1. Authentic smartphone video outperforms polished brand videos on TikTok/Reels because it feels REAL, not produced.

5. TikTok/Reels Strategy: 6 Hook Formats

TikTok and Instagram Reels reward: strong hooks (first 1–2 seconds), relatable content, trending audio, and authentic presentation. The poncho has NATURAL TikTok appeal: the tear-away is oddly satisfying, the garbage-bag comparison is visually striking, the sustainability angle resonates with Gen Z/Millennial runners, and the race-day context is emotionally charged. The 6 hook formats below are proven TikTok structures adapted to the poncho’s strengths.

Format Content Notes
Hook: Problem → Solution Content: “Things marathon runners hate: standing in a cold corral in a garbage bag” [relatable footage] → “But what if it kept you warm AND composted after the race?” [reveal the poncho]. TRENDING AUDIO: use a popular “before/after” or “problem/solution” sound. This format WORKS on TikTok because: it starts with a relatable pain point (scroll-stopping), introduces a novel solution (curiosity), and ends with a product reveal (conversion). Target: 100K+ views if the hook is strong and the audio is trending.
Hook: Oddly Satisfying Content: close-up tear-away demo. The perforation ripping. Slow motion. ASMR audio. No narration needed — let the visual and sound carry it. Text overlay: “The most satisfying thing about marathon morning.” Oddly satisfying content is TikTok’s bread and butter. The tear-away is INHERENTLY satisfying: clean line, consistent force, satisfying rip sound. This format has the highest VIRAL potential of any content type.
Hook: Did You Know? Content: “Did you know 35,000 garbage bags are thrown away at the start of the NYC Marathon? Here’s what we’re doing about it.” [waste stats infographic → product reveal → composting footage]. Text overlay with bold stats. Educational content performs well on TikTok when paired with surprising facts. The waste statistics from P0A are genuinely surprising to most runners — they’ve never quantified the problem.
Hook: Get Ready With Me (GRWM) Content: “Get ready with me for the Chicago Marathon” — race-morning routine. Alarm clock at 4 AM, oatmeal, pinning bib, applying Body Glide, putting on the poncho, heading to the corral. The poncho is ONE element of the routine, not the entire focus. GRWM is a massive TikTok format. The poncho appears naturally within a relatable routine. Viewers who are training for a marathon save this video for their own race prep. The product sells itself through context, not pitch.
Hook: What I Wish I Knew Content: “What I wish I knew before my first marathon: #4 — You’re going to be FREEZING at the start line for 45 minutes. Bring a compostable poncho, not a garbage bag.” Listicle format with the poncho as one tip among 5–7. Advice/listicle content positions the brand as a RUNNING AUTHORITY, not just a product seller. The poncho recommendation is embedded in genuinely useful advice, making it more credible.
Hook: Before/After Content: “Before: [runner shivering in garbage bag at start line]. After: [same runner cozy in poncho, smiling].” Split-screen or transition. End: “And the best part? It composts.” Before/after is a proven TikTok format. The visual contrast between garbage bag (ugly, cold-looking, wasteful) and poncho (sleek, warm, sustainable) is immediately compelling.

5.1 TikTok Posting Strategy

FREQUENCY: 3–5 TikToks per week (higher frequency than Instagram because TikTok’s algorithm favors volume). Each TikTok can also be posted as an Instagram Reel (cross-post saves time). TIMING: post between 6–8 AM (runners scrolling before morning run) or 7–9 PM (evening scroll). Experiment with timing — TikTok’s analytics show when followers are most active. AUDIO: use trending audio sounds. Check TikTok’s “Discover” page daily for trending sounds that fit the content. A poncho video with a trending audio can reach 10–100× the audience of the same video with original audio. HASHTAGS: #marathon #running #raceday #sustainability #compostable #marathontraining #runningtiktok + brand hashtag. 3–5 hashtags per post (TikTok’s algorithm doesn’t benefit from hashtag flooding).

7. Race Day Sustainability Checklist: Lead Magnet

The checklist is a downloadable PDF that captures email addresses. It provides genuine VALUE (practical sustainability tips for race day) while serving as an EMAIL CAPTURE tool and a BRANDING asset. The checklist format is ideal because: runners love checklists (race-day prep is inherently checklist-driven), it’s shareable (running clubs forward it to members), and it positions the brand as a sustainability authority beyond just selling ponchos.

Element Specification
Title Race Day Sustainability Checklist: Run Fast, Leave No Trace
Format PDF, 1 page, double-sided. Front: checklist. Back: brand story + QR code to brand.com + CTA (“Get your compostable poncho: brand.com”). Design: P1B visual identity, Earth Green accent, checkmark icons.
Checklist items PRE-RACE: bring a reusable water bottle, pack your compostable poncho, wear sustainable clothing brands, skip single-use plastic bags for gear. RACE DAY: use the poncho instead of a garbage bag, drop it in the compost bin at the start, refuse single-use cups at aid stations if carrying your own, pick up any litter you see on course. POST-RACE: compost your poncho packaging at home (or recycle), share your sustainable race story on social media (#RaceWarmLeaveNoTrace), leave a review of the poncho, and refer a friend.
Distribution Email capture lead magnet: “Download our free Race Day Sustainability Checklist” (landing page at brand.com/checklist, email required for download). Social share: post the checklist as an Instagram carousel (each item = 1 slide). Print: include in B2B sample kits and expo booth handouts. Partner: offer to running clubs for their members.
Email capture integration Klaviyo form on brand.com/checklist: name + email → triggers welcome series (P6B Section 5). Tag: “checklist-download” for segmentation. Expected: 5–10% of site visitors who see the checklist offer will download, providing 50–200 email sign-ups per month at moderate traffic.

8. Race-Week Email Campaign: 6 Stages

Race-week emails are the highest-converting email content. A runner 2 weeks before their marathon is ACTIVELY preparing: buying gear, planning nutrition, checking weather. An email about start-line warmth at this moment is not spam — it’s helpful and timely. The 6-stage sequence moves from awareness (6 weeks out) through consideration (4 weeks), urgency (2 weeks), last chance (race week), emotional connection (race day), and gratitude + review request (post-race).

Timing Stage Content Segmentation
6 weeks before race AWARENESS Subject: “[Race Name] is 6 weeks away — start planning your race morning.” Content: link to race-specific landing page (P6D Section 3). Reminder: “Have you thought about what you’ll wear at the start line?” Soft CTA: “Check out our [Race Name] Start-Line Guide.” Segment: subscribers in the race city or who have browsed the race-specific landing page. Volume: low (awareness building, not selling yet).
4 weeks before race CONSIDERATION Subject: “4 weeks to [Race Name] — here’s what the pros do at the start line.” Content: tips for race-day warmth (excerpt from Blog 5: 10 Things to Bring). Product mention: “Our compostable poncho is the eco-friendly way to stay warm in the corral.” CTA: “Shop the poncho — arrives in time for [Race Name].” Begin introducing the product as a solution. Not hard-selling yet. The tip-based content provides value first.
2 weeks before race URGENCY Subject: “[Race Name] forecast: [X]°F at the start. Are you ready?” Content: actual weather forecast for race morning (or historical average), FLIR thermal comparison image. CTA: “Order by [date] for guaranteed delivery before [Race Name]. Free shipping on 3-packs.” This is the CONVERSION email. The weather forecast makes the cold REAL. The shipping deadline creates urgency. The FLIR image provides proof. The free shipping removes the last objection.
Race week (Mon–Wed) LAST CHANCE Subject: “Last chance to get your poncho for [Race Name] — order TODAY.” Content: final shipping cutoff reminder. Testimonial from a pilot race runner. CTA: “Order now — arrives by Thursday.” Alternative: “Missed the shipping window? Grab one at our expo booth ([Hall], [Booth #]).” This email converts the last 10–20% of interested subscribers who procrastinated. If the brand has an expo booth: redirect to in-person purchase. If no booth: this is the final digital touchpoint.
Race day EXPERIENCE Subject: “Good luck at [Race Name] today! ” Content: “Wishing all [Race Name] runners an amazing race! If you’re wearing a compostable poncho today, tag us in your corral selfie: @[BRAND] #RaceWarmLeaveNoTrace.” No selling. Pure brand warmth and community. This email builds EMOTIONAL CONNECTION. The runner is nervous, excited, and checking their phone one last time before the start. A warm good-luck message from a brand they’ve purchased from creates loyalty. The UGC request (tag us!) generates race-day content.
Post-race (Day 1) GRATITUDE + REVIEW Subject: “You did it! How was [Race Name]?” Content: congratulations, quick satisfaction survey link (2 min), review request (“Tell other runners about your poncho — leave a review”). CTA: “Leave a review and get 15% off your next order.” The post-race high is the BEST time to collect: feedback, reviews, and UGC. The runner just accomplished something incredible and is emotionally primed to share. Capture this moment.

8.1 Race-Week Email Automation

BUILD IN KLAVIYO: create a “Race-Week Campaign” flow for EACH major target race (NYC, Chicago, Boston, MCM, Philadelphia). Trigger: based on the race date (set as a fixed date in the flow). Audience: subscribers in the race city (geographic segmentation) OR subscribers who have visited the race-specific landing page (behavioral segmentation). Each flow sends 6 emails per the schedule above. ALTERNATIVELY: for Season 1 with limited races, send these as manual campaigns (not automated flows) timed to each race. Manual campaigns allow: last-minute weather forecast updates in the 2-week email, real-time expo booth information in the race-week email, and flexible timing adjustments. Transition to automated flows in Season 2 when the race calendar is predictable and the email list is larger.

10. Conclusion: Content Is the Brand’s Heartbeat

A brand that goes silent between race seasons is forgotten by race season. Content keeps the brand alive in feeds, inboxes, and search results 365 days a year. The Monday sustainability fact reminds a runner that garbage bags are wasteful. The Wednesday ambassador spotlight reminds them that real runners use the poncho. The Friday poll reminds them that the brand is fun, relatable, and human. And when race week arrives and the 2-week email lands with a 42°F forecast, the decision is already made.

The content flywheel: social content builds awareness → awareness drives website traffic → traffic drives email sign-ups (checklist lead magnet) → email nurtures consideration (race-week sequence) → consideration drives purchases → purchases generate reviews + UGC → UGC becomes new social content. Every piece of content feeds the next. The flywheel spins faster with each race season, each new ambassador, and each piece of pilot-race data. By Month 12, the content engine runs on its own momentum: there is always something to post, always a race to prepare for, always a composting story to tell.
